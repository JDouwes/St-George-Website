Lyme disease skin symptoms

Reviewed by Julian Douwes M.D., Chief Medical Officer, Klinik St. Georg

The skin is often the first organ to show signs of Lyme disease and, in late-stage European Lyme, one of the last to resolve. From the characteristic bull's-eye rash to chronic skin changes that develop years after infection, cutaneous manifestations of Lyme disease are clinically important diagnostic clues that are frequently overlooked.

At a glance

Erythema migrans: The hallmark bull's-eye rash, present in approximately 70% of confirmed acute Lyme cases. Its presence is diagnostic; its absence does not rule out infection.

Late-stage skin signs: Acrodermatitis chronica atrophicans (ACA) and borrelial lymphocytoma are European Lyme manifestations that can appear months to years after infection.

Co-infection clues: Bartonella co-infection can produce distinctive skin findings, including linear stretch-mark-like lesions, that may indicate a more complex tick-borne illness.

Erythema migrans: the bull's-eye rash

Erythema migrans (EM) is the most recognized sign of early Lyme disease and the single most reliable clinical indicator of acute Borrelia infection. When present, it is considered diagnostic and treatment should begin immediately, without waiting for laboratory confirmation.

Clinical appearance: The classic presentation is an expanding, circular or oval red lesion that develops at the site of the tick bite, typically 3 to 30 days after the bite. In textbook form, it features a central clearing that creates a "target" or "bull's-eye" pattern. However, many EM lesions present as uniformly red, oval patches without central clearing. The lesion is usually flat or minimally raised, warm to the touch but not typically tender or itchy, and expands gradually over days to weeks. It commonly reaches 5 centimeters or larger in diameter; some lesions grow to 30 centimeters or more.

Where it appears: EM develops at the site of the tick bite, which is often in areas the patient may not easily see: the back, groin, armpit, behind the knee, scalp, or buttocks. This is one reason why many patients never notice the rash.

Important facts:

  • EM is present in approximately 70% of confirmed Lyme cases, meaning up to 30% of patients never develop a rash at all
  • The rash may be more difficult to identify on darker skin tones, where it can appear as a bruise-like discoloration rather than a bright red ring
  • Multiple EM lesions at different sites indicate early disseminated infection, as Borrelia has spread through the bloodstream
  • EM should not be confused with the small, localized redness that occurs at many tick bite sites as a simple irritation reaction (which typically appears within hours and is smaller than 5 cm)

Acrodermatitis chronica atrophicans (ACA)

Acrodermatitis chronica atrophicans is a late-stage skin manifestation of Lyme disease seen almost exclusively in European Lyme cases, where it is caused by Borrelia afzelii, the predominant European Borrelia species. ACA is rare in North America.

Clinical appearance: ACA typically develops on the extremities, most commonly the dorsal surfaces of the hands and feet, and the extensor surfaces of the forearms and lower legs. In its early inflammatory phase, the skin appears swollen and has a dusky red or violaceous (purplish) discoloration. Over months to years, the skin transitions to an atrophic phase: it becomes paper-thin, translucent, and wrinkled, with visible underlying veins and tendons. The texture has been compared to tissue paper or cigarette paper. The affected areas may feel cool to the touch.

Diagnostic significance: ACA is virtually pathognomonic for late-stage Borrelia infection. Its presence indicates that the infection has been active for months to years, even if the patient does not recall a tick bite or an EM rash. ACA is sometimes the presenting sign that leads to a first diagnosis of Lyme disease in patients who have been symptomatic for years without explanation.

Treatment response: ACA in its inflammatory phase typically responds to prolonged antibiotic treatment. In the atrophic phase, the skin changes may be partially or fully irreversible, though halting the infection prevents further progression.

Borrelial lymphocytoma

Borrelial lymphocytoma is a relatively rare skin manifestation of Lyme disease, more commonly reported in European cases, particularly in children.

Clinical appearance: It presents as a solitary, bluish-red or violaceous nodule, typically 1 to 5 centimeters in diameter. It is firm, non-tender, and painless. In adults, it most commonly appears on the earlobe, nipple, or areola. In children, it is most frequently found on the earlobe. The nodule represents a localized accumulation of B-lymphocytes in response to Borrelia infection.

Diagnostic considerations: Borrelial lymphocytoma can be mistaken for a skin tumor, particularly lymphoma. Biopsy shows a dense lymphocytic infiltrate that, without clinical context, can raise concern for malignancy. Awareness of this entity in the context of tick exposure is important to avoid unnecessary oncological workup.

Borrelial lymphocytoma typically resolves with appropriate antibiotic treatment for Lyme disease.

Other skin manifestations

Beyond the three classic dermatological presentations, Lyme disease has been associated with several other skin findings, though these are less specific and less well-documented:

Urticaria-like lesions: Some patients report hive-like eruptions during acute or early disseminated Lyme disease. These are thought to represent an immune-mediated response rather than direct bacterial invasion of the skin.

Morphea-like lesions: Localized scleroderma-like plaques have been reported in association with Borrelia infection, particularly in European case reports. These present as firm, discolored patches of thickened skin. The causal relationship between Borrelia and morphea remains debated, but the association has been documented in multiple case series.

Panniculitis: Inflammation of subcutaneous fat tissue has been reported in rare cases of Lyme disease, presenting as tender nodules beneath the skin surface.

Bartonella co-infection skin signs

Bartonella is a common tick-borne co-infection that produces distinctive skin findings of its own. Recognizing these can be an important clue that a patient's tick-borne illness extends beyond Borrelia alone.

Stretch-mark-like lesions (striae): The most characteristic Bartonella skin sign is the development of linear, red or purple stretch-mark-like lesions that appear in locations not typically associated with weight change or growth spurts. These lesions may appear on the trunk, inner arms, thighs, or back. They are often described as resembling fresh striae but occurring in anatomically unusual distributions. While not all physicians accept this association, it is widely recognized in Lyme-literate clinical practice.

Subcutaneous nodules: Small, tender nodules beneath the skin, particularly on the soles of the feet, have been associated with Bartonella infection.

Cherry angiomas: Some clinicians have noted an increased prevalence of small, bright-red vascular lesions (cherry angiomas) in patients with Bartonella co-infection, though this association is clinical observation rather than established science.

When skin symptoms are absent

It is essential to understand that the absence of skin symptoms does not rule out Lyme disease. Approximately 30% of patients with confirmed Lyme disease never develop erythema migrans. Late-stage skin manifestations like ACA are specific to certain Borrelia species and geographic regions. Many patients present with systemic symptoms (fatigue, joint pain, neurological complaints) without any dermatological findings at all.

The absence of a rash is one of the most common reasons for delayed or missed Lyme diagnosis. Patients and physicians alike may dismiss the possibility of Lyme disease when no rash is recalled, despite the fact that the rash is absent in a significant minority of cases.

If you are experiencing systemic symptoms consistent with Lyme disease but have never had a visible rash, this does not mean you do not have Lyme disease. It means you need a thorough clinical evaluation that does not depend on rash history alone.

Related symptoms

Joint pain

Migratory joint pain, stiffness, and inflammation that may shift between joints and vary in intensity over time.

Nerve pain

Neuropathic symptoms including tingling, numbness, burning sensations, and shooting pain.

Chronic fatigue

Persistent, debilitating exhaustion that does not improve with rest and is not explained by other conditions.
