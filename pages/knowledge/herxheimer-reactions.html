What is a Herxheimer reaction?

The Jarisch-Herxheimer reaction — commonly called a "Herx" or "die-off reaction" — is a temporary worsening of symptoms that occurs when large numbers of bacteria are killed during treatment. It was first described in the late 19th century by dermatologists Adolf Jarisch and Karl Herxheimer, who observed it in syphilis patients treated with mercury-based therapies. Both syphilis and Lyme disease are caused by spirochete bacteria, and the Herxheimer reaction occurs in both conditions.

When Borrelia bacteria are destroyed by antibiotics, hyperthermia, or immune activity, they release fragments of their cell walls — particularly lipoproteins and endotoxin-like molecules — into the bloodstream and surrounding tissues. The immune system responds to this sudden influx of bacterial debris with a surge of inflammatory cytokines, producing a temporary but sometimes intense inflammatory response that makes the patient feel significantly worse before they feel better.

Understanding this reaction is important because it is one of the most common causes of fear and discouragement during Lyme treatment. Patients who are not warned about Herxheimer reactions may believe their treatment is failing or causing harm, when in many cases the reaction is evidence that bacteria are being killed effectively.

What does a Herxheimer reaction feel like?

The symptoms of a Herxheimer reaction typically mirror and intensify the patient's existing Lyme symptoms, along with additional systemic responses:

  • Intensified fatigue: A deep, bone-level exhaustion that goes beyond the patient's baseline fatigue
  • Fever and chills: Low-grade fever (37.5–38.5°C) is common; higher fevers are possible but less typical
  • Increased joint and muscle pain: Pain in areas that were already symptomatic, often noticeably worse than baseline
  • Brain fog spike: Worsened cognitive function, increased difficulty concentrating and processing information
  • Headache: Often described as a dull, pressure-like headache distinct from tension headaches
  • Night sweats: Can be profuse and disruptive to sleep
  • Mood changes: Increased anxiety, irritability, or depressive feelings
  • Skin reactions: Flushing, rashes, or reactivation of previous erythema migrans sites
  • Gastrointestinal symptoms: Nausea, bloating, or changes in bowel patterns
  • Neurological symptoms: Tingling, numbness, or worsened nerve pain in patients with neurological Lyme
Patients often describe a Herxheimer reaction as "my worst Lyme day, concentrated into a few hours." It is unpleasant, sometimes frightening, but in most cases self-limiting and manageable.

Why it happens during effective treatment

A Herxheimer reaction is, paradoxically, a sign that treatment is reaching its target. It occurs because effective antimicrobial therapy is killing bacteria faster than the body can clear the resulting debris. Several factors make Herxheimer reactions particularly common and sometimes intense during Lyme treatment:

Whole-body hyperthermia

Hyperthermia is especially likely to trigger a Herxheimer reaction because it kills bacteria rapidly across multiple body compartments simultaneously. Unlike antibiotics, which reach different tissues at different concentrations, elevated core temperature affects bacteria systemically. This means that a larger volume of bacterial die-off occurs in a shorter time window, producing a more concentrated inflammatory response.

Combination therapy

When hyperthermia is combined with antibiotics — as is standard practice at Klinik St. Georg — the antimicrobial effect is synergistic. Hyperthermia disrupts the structural integrity of Borrelia while simultaneously making the bacteria more susceptible to antibiotics. This dual assault can produce a more pronounced Herxheimer reaction than either treatment alone.

High bacterial burden

Patients with long-standing, untreated, or undertreated Lyme disease may harbor a higher load of bacteria across multiple tissue sites. When treatment is initiated, the sheer volume of bacterial die-off can produce a more intense Herxheimer response. In our clinical experience, patients who have the most significant Herxheimer reactions often also have the most significant long-term improvement.

How long does it last?

A typical Herxheimer reaction follows a predictable pattern:

  • Onset: Usually begins within hours of treatment initiation, though it can be delayed by 12–24 hours
  • Peak intensity: Most patients experience the worst symptoms within the first 24 hours
  • Resolution: The majority of Herxheimer reactions resolve within 24–72 hours. By day 3–5, most patients report feeling at least as well as they did before treatment, and many report feeling better than their pre-treatment baseline
  • Subsequent cycles: With repeated treatment sessions, Herxheimer reactions typically become less intense as the bacterial burden decreases. This diminishing intensity is itself an indicator of treatment progress

What patients often tell us: "The first day after hyperthermia was the worst day of my treatment. By day three, I felt better than I had in months." This pattern — a brief, intense worsening followed by sustained improvement — is one of the most characteristic features of effective Lyme treatment.

How Klinik St. Georg manages Herxheimer reactions

We do not view Herxheimer reactions as an unfortunate side effect to be endured. They are an expected part of the treatment process, and managing them effectively is integral to patient care:

Pre-treatment preparation

Before initiating hyperthermia or intensive antibiotic therapy, we prepare patients with hydration protocols, supportive supplementation, and detoxification support to help the body process the expected release of bacterial debris. We also educate patients thoroughly about what to expect, so that a Herxheimer reaction does not come as a frightening surprise.

During treatment

Patients undergoing whole-body hyperthermia are continuously monitored by physicians and nursing staff. Intravenous hydration supports fluid balance and assists in clearing inflammatory mediators. Anti-inflammatory support can be provided when symptoms are particularly intense.

Post-treatment support

After treatment, detoxification support continues. This may include alkaline infusions, liver support, binder protocols, and lymphatic drainage to help the body clear the inflammatory debris. Adequate rest, hydration, and nutritional support are emphasized. Patients remain under medical observation throughout their stay at the clinic.

Herxheimer reaction vs. other causes of symptom worsening

Not every worsening of symptoms during Lyme treatment is a Herxheimer reaction. It is important to distinguish between:

Herxheimer reaction

  • Occurs within hours to days of effective antimicrobial treatment
  • Symptoms mirror and intensify existing Lyme symptoms
  • Self-limiting, typically resolving within 24–72 hours
  • Followed by a period of improvement relative to pre-treatment baseline
  • Decreases in intensity with subsequent treatment cycles

Medication side effects

  • Related to specific medications rather than bacterial die-off
  • Symptoms may be new rather than intensifications of existing ones (e.g., photosensitivity with doxycycline)
  • Persistent as long as the medication continues
  • Resolves when the medication is discontinued or changed

Disease progression

  • Gradual worsening over weeks to months, not the acute time course of a Herxheimer reaction
  • Not temporally related to treatment initiation
  • May include new symptoms not present at baseline
  • Does not self-resolve within days

When should you be concerned?

While Herxheimer reactions are generally benign and self-limiting, patients should contact their treating physician if:

  • Symptoms are severe enough to significantly impair breathing, consciousness, or cardiovascular function
  • High fever (>39°C / 102.2°F) persists for more than 24 hours
  • Symptoms do not begin to improve after 72 hours
  • New symptoms develop that were not part of the patient's previous symptom profile
  • The patient feels that something is fundamentally different from previous Herxheimer reactions

Important distinction: A Herxheimer reaction is uncomfortable but temporary. If symptoms are worsening progressively rather than peaking and resolving, or if entirely new and concerning symptoms appear, contact your treating physician promptly. Patient intuition — "this feels different" — should always be taken seriously.
