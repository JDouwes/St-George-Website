The mold and Lyme connection

Reviewed by Julian Douwes M.D., Chief Medical Officer, Klinik St. Georg

Many chronic Lyme patients are also affected by mold illness, and the overlap is not coincidental. Mold exposure and Lyme disease share biotoxin pathways, produce nearly identical symptoms, and can prevent recovery from one another. If you have been treating Lyme without improvement, unrecognized mold exposure may be part of the reason.

At a glance

CIRS: Chronic Inflammatory Response Syndrome is a multi-system inflammatory condition triggered by biotoxins, including those from mold and from Borrelia burgdorferi.

The overlap: Approximately 25% of the population carries HLA gene types that impair their ability to clear biotoxins. These individuals are susceptible to both mold illness and chronic Lyme disease.

Why it matters: Ongoing mold exposure can sustain the inflammatory cascade even after Lyme treatment, preventing recovery. Both must be addressed for treatment to succeed.

What is CIRS?

Chronic Inflammatory Response Syndrome (CIRS) is a condition originally described by Dr. Ritchie Shoemaker in which the innate immune system is chronically activated by biotoxins that the body cannot properly eliminate. These biotoxins can originate from water-damaged buildings (mold and its mycotoxins), from tick-borne infections (Borrelia, Babesia), from cyanobacteria, and from other biological sources.

In a normally functioning immune system, biotoxins are tagged, processed, and eliminated. In susceptible individuals, a genetic variation in the HLA-DR gene impairs this clearance process. The biotoxins remain in circulation, continuously triggering inflammatory cytokines and creating a self-perpetuating cycle of inflammation that affects virtually every organ system.

CIRS symptoms overlap extensively with chronic Lyme:

  • Fatigue and post-exertional malaise
  • Cognitive dysfunction (brain fog, memory problems, word-finding difficulty)
  • Joint and muscle pain
  • Headaches
  • Shortness of breath and air hunger
  • Light sensitivity and visual disturbances
  • Temperature dysregulation
  • Sleep disturbance
  • Abdominal symptoms

The symptom overlap is so extensive that mold illness and chronic Lyme disease are frequently confused with each other, or one is diagnosed while the other is missed entirely.

Why mold illness and Lyme often coexist

The connection between mold illness and Lyme disease is not merely coincidental. There are several reasons these conditions frequently occur together:

Shared genetic susceptibility. The same HLA gene variants that predispose individuals to mold illness also impair clearance of Borrelia-derived biotoxins. An estimated 24% of the general population carries a "mold-susceptible" HLA type, and a subset of these individuals carry what Shoemaker termed the "dreaded genotype," which predisposes to both mold illness and chronic Lyme simultaneously.

Cumulative biotoxin burden. Think of the body's biotoxin clearance capacity as a cup. Mold exposure fills the cup to a certain level. Borrelia infection fills it further. At some point, the cup overflows, and the immune system can no longer manage the cumulative load. A patient who might have tolerated either mold exposure or Lyme infection alone may be overwhelmed by both together.

Immune suppression. Chronic mold exposure suppresses certain arms of the immune system while overactivating others. This dysregulated immune state can make it harder for the body to fight Borrelia, potentially allowing a Lyme infection to become chronic that might otherwise have been cleared. Conversely, chronic Lyme infection impairs the immune system's ability to manage mold biotoxins.

Shared inflammatory pathways. Both mold biotoxins and Borrelia-derived toxins trigger TGF-beta, C4a, MMP-9, and other inflammatory markers through overlapping pathways. The result is compounded inflammation that is greater than either trigger alone.

How mold exposure prevents Lyme recovery

This is the most clinically important point on this page: if you are being treated for Lyme disease but continue to live or work in a mold-contaminated environment, your treatment may not produce lasting results.

Persistent inflammatory signaling. Even if Lyme treatment successfully reduces the Borrelia burden, ongoing mold exposure continues to drive the same inflammatory cascades. The patient experiences no relief because the inflammation never resolves.

Immune exhaustion. A chronically activated immune system loses its ability to mount effective targeted responses. This can impair the efficacy of antimicrobial treatment, whether pharmaceutical or herbal.

Detoxification pathway overload. The liver, kidneys, and lymphatic system have finite processing capacity. During Lyme treatment, particularly during Herxheimer-like die-off reactions, these systems are already under significant strain. Add ongoing mycotoxin processing and the detoxification system may be overwhelmed, leading to worsening symptoms rather than improvement.

Symptom attribution confusion. Because mold illness and Lyme symptoms are nearly identical, ongoing mold symptoms may be attributed to treatment failure, leading to unnecessary changes in antimicrobial strategy when the real problem is environmental.

Environmental assessment and remediation

For chronic Lyme patients who are not improving as expected, environmental assessment for mold should be considered. This is not fringe medicine. Water damage affects an estimated 50% of buildings, and many patients are unaware that their home or workplace harbors significant mold contamination.

Assessment approaches:

  • ERMI testing (Environmental Relative Moldiness Index) uses dust sampling to quantify mold species and concentrations in a building.
  • HERTSMI-2 is a focused version of ERMI that tests specifically for the five mold species most commonly associated with CIRS.
  • Professional inspection by a qualified indoor environmental professional (IEP) can identify hidden water damage and mold growth that are not visible during a standard home inspection.
  • Urinary mycotoxin testing can indicate whether a patient is currently being exposed to and absorbing mold-derived toxins, though interpretation of these tests requires clinical expertise.

Remediation is essential, not optional. If significant mold contamination is identified, professional remediation must be performed before meaningful Lyme treatment progress can be expected. In severe cases, relocation may be necessary. This is a difficult reality, but no amount of antimicrobial therapy will overcome ongoing environmental toxin exposure.

Treatment sequence: addressing mold alongside Lyme

For patients dealing with both mold illness and Lyme disease, the treatment approach must address both conditions, ideally in a coordinated sequence:

Step 1: Remove the source. Environmental remediation or relocation must come first. Treating while remaining in a contaminated environment is inefficient at best and futile at worst.

Step 2: Biotoxin binding. Cholestyramine (CSM) or other binding agents can help sequester circulating biotoxins and reduce the inflammatory burden. This is often the first medical intervention in mold illness treatment.

Step 3: Reduce inflammation. Anti-inflammatory interventions, dietary changes, and targeted supplements help bring down the systemic inflammatory response that both mold and Lyme have been driving.

Step 4: Address Lyme infection. Once the mold burden is reduced and the body's detoxification pathways are less overwhelmed, Lyme treatment, whether antibiotics, hyperthermia, or a combination, is more likely to be effective.

Step 5: Monitor and adjust. Recovery from dual mold-Lyme illness is rarely linear. Regular monitoring of inflammatory markers, symptom tracking, and ongoing attention to environmental quality are essential throughout the recovery process.

The most important thing a patient with both mold illness and Lyme disease can understand is that these are not two separate problems requiring two separate solutions. They are interconnected conditions that share biological pathways and must be addressed together.
