How ticks find their hosts

Understanding tick behavior is the first step toward effective prevention. Ticks do not jump, fly, or drop from trees. They use a hunting strategy called questing: they climb to the tips of grasses, shrubs, or leaf litter, extend their front legs, and wait for a host to brush past.

Ticks detect approaching hosts through sophisticated sensory organs. They can sense carbon dioxide from your breath at distances of several meters. They detect body heat, moisture, vibrations, and even the scent of ammonia and other compounds in sweat. A specialized sensory structure on their front legs called Haller's organ allows them to detect these chemical signals with remarkable precision.

This is why ticks are most commonly picked up on the lower legs and ankles — you walk through the vegetation where they are questing. From there, they crawl upward, often settling in warm, hidden areas: behind the knees, in the groin, at the waistline, in the armpits, behind the ears, and along the hairline.

Nymph-stage ticks: the invisible threat

The nymphal stage of the tick lifecycle is responsible for the majority of Lyme disease transmissions. Nymphs are roughly the size of a poppy seed — approximately 1.5 millimeters — and their bites are almost always painless. Most patients who develop Lyme disease never saw or felt the tick that infected them. This is not a failure of vigilance; nymphs are genuinely difficult to detect even with careful inspection.

Prevention strategies

Clothing as a barrier

Clothing is your first line of defense. When walking in tick habitat — forests, tall grass, leaf litter, meadow edges — wear long trousers tucked into socks or boots, long-sleeved shirts, and closed-toe shoes. Light-colored clothing makes ticks easier to spot before they reach your skin. While this may feel impractical in warm weather, even thin, breathable fabrics provide a meaningful barrier.

Chemical repellents

  • DEET (20–30% concentration): Applied to exposed skin, DEET is the most widely studied tick repellent. It provides several hours of protection per application. Higher concentrations last longer but do not repel more effectively. Reapply after swimming or heavy sweating.
  • Picaridin (20% concentration): An effective alternative to DEET with less odor and less damage to synthetic fabrics. Comparable tick repellency in controlled studies.
  • Permethrin (clothing treatment): Permethrin is not applied to skin — it is applied to clothing, shoes, and gear. It kills ticks on contact and remains effective through several washes. Pre-treated clothing is also commercially available. Permethrin-treated clothing combined with DEET on exposed skin is the most effective repellent strategy available.
  • Oil of lemon eucalyptus (OLE/PMD): The only plant-derived repellent recommended by the CDC for tick protection, though it provides shorter duration than DEET or picaridin and should not be used on children under 3 years.

Tick checks: the most important habit

No repellent is 100% effective. The single most important prevention measure is a thorough tick check after spending time outdoors. Ticks typically crawl for 1–2 hours before attaching, and Borrelia transmission generally requires 24–48 hours of tick attachment (though shorter transmission times have been documented in some studies). This means you have a window of opportunity to find and remove ticks before infection occurs.

After coming indoors, shower within two hours and conduct a systematic, full-body check. Use a mirror or ask a partner to check areas you cannot see. Pay particular attention to:

  • Behind and around the ears, along the hairline and scalp
  • Under the arms, inside the navel, around the waist
  • Behind the knees, between the toes, in the groin area
  • On children: the scalp and hairline are especially common attachment sites

Tumble-dry clothing on high heat for 10 minutes to kill any ticks that may be on your clothes. Washing alone, even in hot water, is not reliably effective at killing ticks.

Proper tick removal

If you find an attached tick, correct removal technique matters. The goal is to remove the entire tick, including the mouthparts, as quickly and cleanly as possible without squeezing the tick's body.

Correct technique

  1. Use fine-tipped tweezers (not blunt household tweezers). Tick removal tools are also effective.
  2. Grasp the tick as close to the skin surface as possible — at the mouthparts, not the body.
  3. Pull upward with steady, even pressure. Do not twist, jerk, or yank. The tick will release.
  4. If mouthparts break off and remain in the skin, try to remove them with the tweezers. If you cannot remove them easily, leave them and let the skin heal — the mouthparts alone cannot transmit infection.
  5. Clean the bite area with rubbing alcohol or soap and water. Wash your hands.
  6. Save the tick in a sealed bag or container with the date recorded. This can be useful if symptoms develop later.

What NOT to do: Do not apply nail polish, petroleum jelly, alcohol, or heat (matches, cigarettes) to an attached tick. These methods do not cause the tick to detach and may cause it to regurgitate its gut contents into the wound, potentially increasing the risk of pathogen transmission. Do not squeeze or crush the tick's body during removal.

When to seek medical attention

Not every tick bite leads to Lyme disease. The risk depends on the tick species, the region, the duration of attachment, and whether the tick was carrying Borrelia. However, knowing when to seek medical evaluation is important.

Seek medical attention if:

  • An expanding rash appears at or near the bite site (erythema migrans), typically 3–30 days after the bite. The rash may or may not have a bull's-eye pattern.
  • You develop flu-like symptoms (fever, chills, fatigue, headache, muscle or joint pain) within 30 days of a tick bite, especially during tick season.
  • You cannot remove the tick completely or are unsure about proper removal.
  • The tick was attached for more than 24 hours or you are unsure how long it was attached.
  • You are in a high-risk geographic area and want to discuss prophylactic antibiotic treatment with your doctor.

Important: The absence of a rash does not rule out Lyme infection. Up to 30% of confirmed Lyme cases never develop a visible erythema migrans rash. If you develop unexplained symptoms in the weeks following a tick bite, seek medical evaluation regardless of whether a rash appeared. Learn more about Lyme testing and diagnosis.

Geographic risk areas

Lyme disease is a global concern, not limited to any single region. Understanding where risk is highest helps you calibrate your prevention efforts.

Europe

Central Europe carries the highest European risk, particularly Germany, Austria, Switzerland, the Czech Republic, Slovenia, and the Baltic states. In Germany alone, an estimated 200,000–300,000 new Lyme infections occur annually. Southern Scandinavia, the UK, France, and parts of Eastern Europe also carry significant risk. The primary vector is Ixodes ricinus, and the Borrelia species landscape is more diverse than in North America, with B. afzelii and B. garinii alongside B. burgdorferi.

North America

The highest-risk regions are the US Northeast (Connecticut to Maine), the upper Midwest (Wisconsin, Minnesota), and the mid-Atlantic states. The Pacific Coast, particularly Northern California and the Pacific Northwest, carries moderate risk. The primary vector is Ixodes scapularis (blacklegged tick) in the East and Midwest, and Ixodes pacificus on the Pacific Coast. Over 476,000 cases per year are estimated in the United States by recent CDC models.

Asia and beyond

Lyme borreliosis is documented across northern China, Japan, South Korea, and Russia. The true global burden is likely underestimated due to varying surveillance and diagnostic standards. If you live in or travel to any temperate forested region of the Northern Hemisphere, tick prevention should be part of your outdoor routine.

Seasonal risk

Peak tick activity in the Northern Hemisphere runs from April through October, with nymphs most active from May through July — the period of highest Lyme disease transmission. Adult ticks are active in autumn and early spring, and in regions with mild winters (including much of Central Europe), adult ticks can remain active through the winter months.

Climate change is extending tick seasons and expanding tick habitats into previously low-risk areas, including higher altitudes and more northern latitudes. The assumption that winter eliminates tick risk is increasingly unreliable.

The bottom line: tick prevention is not a summer-only concern. Any time you are outdoors in tick habitat and temperatures are above approximately 4°C (40°F), tick checks and appropriate precautions are warranted.
