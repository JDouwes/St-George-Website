The brain is not separate from the body

If you are living with Lyme disease and struggling with depression, anxiety, or cognitive difficulties, we want you to hear something clearly: these are not separate conditions. They are not evidence that you are mentally weak, that you cannot cope, or that your physical symptoms are psychosomatic. They are neurological manifestations of an infection that has reached your central nervous system.

Borrelia burgdorferi has a documented affinity for nervous tissue. It can cross the blood-brain barrier, trigger neuroinflammation, disrupt neurotransmitter production, and impair the function of neural pathways that regulate mood, cognition, and emotional stability. When patients with Lyme disease develop psychiatric symptoms, the most parsimonious explanation is usually the simplest one: the infection is affecting the brain.

This is not a fringe position. Neuroborreliosis — Lyme disease affecting the nervous system — is a well-documented clinical entity recognized by mainstream infectious disease medicine. The controversy is not about whether Lyme can affect the brain. It is about how often it does, how long the effects last, and what to do about it.

Psychiatric symptoms of Lyme disease

The range of psychiatric and cognitive symptoms associated with Lyme disease is broader than most patients or physicians expect:

Depression

Not ordinary sadness or situational depression (though being chronically ill is certainly depressing). Lyme-associated depression is often characterized by a flatness, a loss of the ability to feel pleasure or motivation, that patients describe as fundamentally different from any depression they have experienced before. It can be profound, sudden in onset, and resistant to standard antidepressant therapy. Research suggests this depression is driven by neuroinflammatory cytokines that directly alter serotonin and dopamine metabolism.

Anxiety and panic

Many Lyme patients develop anxiety that appears out of nowhere — free-floating dread, heart racing, a sense of impending doom that has no connection to external circumstances. Panic attacks may occur for the first time in a person's life, sometimes in the middle of the night. These are not caused by psychological stress. They are caused by infection-driven disruption of the autonomic nervous system and neurotransmitter balance.

Cognitive impairment

Often called "Lyme brain fog," cognitive impairment is frequently the most distressing symptom patients report. It can include difficulty finding words, trouble following conversations, inability to read and retain information, impaired short-term memory, disorientation, and a pervasive sense that thinking requires enormous effort. For patients who were previously sharp and professionally capable, this cognitive decline can be devastating.

Rage and irritability

Uncharacteristic rage, explosive irritability, and a dramatically shortened fuse are reported by many Lyme patients and their families. A patient who was previously calm and patient may find themselves erupting over minor provocations, frightened by the intensity of their own reactions. This is not a character flaw. Neuroinflammation in the limbic system — the brain's emotional regulation center — can directly impair the ability to modulate emotional responses.

OCD-like symptoms and intrusive thoughts

Some patients develop obsessive thought patterns, compulsive behaviors, or disturbing intrusive thoughts that resemble obsessive-compulsive disorder. In children, the overlap with PANDAS/PANS (Pediatric Autoimmune Neuropsychiatric Disorders Associated with Infections) has been documented. These symptoms can emerge abruptly and may resolve when the underlying infection is treated.

Depersonalization and derealization

A feeling of being disconnected from oneself or from reality — watching your own life as if from outside your body, or feeling that the world around you is not quite real — is reported by some patients with neuroborreliosis. These experiences are deeply unsettling, and patients are often reluctant to describe them for fear of being labelled as psychiatrically unstable.

The dismissal problem

Perhaps the most damaging aspect of the Lyme-mental health connection is how often it leads to misdiagnosis and dismissal.

The pattern is painfully common: a patient develops a constellation of physical and psychological symptoms. They see their general practitioner, who may order basic blood work that comes back normal. They are referred to a psychiatrist, who diagnoses depression or anxiety disorder based on symptom presentation alone. They are prescribed antidepressants or anxiolytics. The underlying infection is never investigated.

The phrase patients hear most often is some variation of: "It's all in your head." The tragic irony is that in a very literal sense, it is in their head — an infection has reached their brain. But the phrase is used to invalidate, not to diagnose.

When a patient who was previously emotionally stable develops sudden-onset depression, anxiety, rage, and cognitive impairment alongside physical symptoms like fatigue, joint pain, and neurological complaints, the question should not be "What psychiatric condition explains this?" It should be "What systemic process is affecting this patient's brain?"

This dismissal is not merely frustrating. It is medically dangerous. Months and years pass while the underlying infection progresses. The patient deteriorates. Relationships strain or break. Careers are lost. And throughout, the patient is told that their suffering is a mental health problem requiring psychological intervention rather than a medical problem requiring medical treatment.

When psychiatric medication masks infection

Psychiatric medications can sometimes partially alleviate the psychological symptoms of Lyme disease. An SSRI may take the edge off depression. A benzodiazepine may reduce anxiety. But if these medications are prescribed without investigating the underlying cause, they can create a dangerous illusion of treatment while the infection continues unchecked.

Some specific concerns:

  • Partial symptom relief delays diagnosis: If antidepressants slightly improve mood, both patient and physician may conclude that the problem is "just depression," reducing the urgency to investigate further.
  • Side effects compound existing symptoms: Many psychiatric medications cause fatigue, cognitive dulling, weight changes, and sleep disruption — symptoms that overlap with and can worsen Lyme disease symptoms.
  • Medication changes obscure the clinical picture: Repeated medication adjustments and additions make it increasingly difficult to distinguish drug effects from disease progression.
  • The underlying infection progresses: While symptoms are being managed pharmacologically, Borrelia may be disseminating further into the nervous system and other tissues.

This is not an argument against psychiatric medication in Lyme patients. Some patients genuinely benefit from psychiatric support during treatment and recovery. It is an argument against psychiatric medication instead of medical investigation and treatment of the underlying cause.

An integrated approach

At Klinik St. Georg, we believe that effective treatment of Lyme-associated psychiatric symptoms requires addressing both the infection and its neurological consequences:

Treat the infection

The foundation of treatment is addressing the Borrelia infection itself. Whole-body hyperthermia, targeted antibiotics, and immune support therapies aim to reduce the bacterial burden driving neuroinflammation. Many patients report significant improvement in psychiatric symptoms after effective antimicrobial treatment — further evidence that these symptoms are infection-driven.

Reduce neuroinflammation

Anti-inflammatory strategies, including H.E.L.P. apheresis to clear circulating immune complexes and inflammatory mediators, can help calm the neuroinflammatory cascade that drives psychiatric symptoms.

Support neurological recovery

Nutritional support for brain health, mitochondrial optimization, and targeted supplementation can support neurological recovery as the infection is treated. The brain has remarkable capacity for healing when the underlying insult is addressed.

Provide psychological support

Living with chronic Lyme disease is psychologically demanding even beyond the direct neurological effects of infection. The experience of being dismissed, doubted, and medically abandoned takes its own toll. Compassionate psychological support — from professionals who understand that the patient is medically ill — is a valuable complement to medical treatment. Psychiatric medication may be appropriate in some cases, but it should be part of a comprehensive plan, not a substitute for treating the infection.

A note to anyone struggling

If you are reading this because you are experiencing these symptoms, we want you to know:

You are not losing your mind. Your brain is responding to a medical insult. The depression, the anxiety, the fog, the rage — these are symptoms of a treatable condition, not evidence of personal weakness or psychiatric fragility.

You are not alone. We have treated thousands of patients who have described exactly what you are experiencing. The pattern is so consistent that we recognize it within minutes of hearing a patient's story.

You deserve to be taken seriously. If a physician has told you that your symptoms are psychological without thoroughly investigating medical causes, that is a failure of their diagnostic process, not a reflection of your reality.
