What are biofilms?

A biofilm is a structured community of bacteria enclosed within a self-produced protective matrix. Think of it as a fortress built by bacteria to protect themselves from hostile environments — including the human immune system and antibiotic medications.

Biofilms are not unique to Lyme disease. They are among the most ancient and successful survival strategies in biology. The National Institutes of Health estimates that biofilms are involved in up to 80% of all chronic bacterial infections, including chronic urinary tract infections, chronic wound infections, endocarditis, and prosthetic joint infections.

What makes biofilms so significant is that bacteria within a biofilm behave fundamentally differently from their free-floating (planktonic) counterparts. They slow their metabolism, alter their gene expression, communicate with each other through chemical signaling, and collectively produce a matrix of polysaccharides, proteins, and DNA that forms a physical barrier against external threats.

How Borrelia forms biofilms

Research has demonstrated that Borrelia burgdorferi is capable of forming biofilms both in laboratory settings and in human tissue. This finding, first reported by Sapi et al. in 2012 and subsequently confirmed by multiple research groups, has significant implications for understanding why Lyme disease can be so difficult to eradicate.

The formation process

Borrelia biofilm formation follows a well-characterized progression:

  1. Attachment. Individual spirochetes attach to a surface — which could be tissue, collagen, or other extracellular matrix components in the body.
  2. Aggregation. Multiple spirochetes gather together and begin to organize into a structured community.
  3. Matrix production. The aggregated bacteria secrete a protective extracellular matrix composed of polysaccharides, proteins, DNA, and calcium. This matrix encases the bacterial community.
  4. Maturation. The biofilm develops into a complex, three-dimensional structure with channels for nutrient and waste transport. Different regions of the biofilm contain bacteria in different metabolic states.
  5. Dispersal. Under certain conditions, bacteria can be released from the biofilm to colonize new sites, potentially explaining symptom relapses and disease spread.

Where biofilms form in the body

Borrelia biofilms have been identified in multiple tissue types, including skin, synovial tissue (joints), the central nervous system, and within the walls of blood vessels. Their formation in deep tissue compartments is particularly problematic because these are areas where both immune surveillance and antibiotic penetration are already limited.

Why biofilms make standard antibiotics less effective

The presence of biofilms is one of the most compelling explanations for why standard antibiotic therapy fails to eliminate Lyme disease in a significant percentage of patients. Multiple mechanisms contribute to this treatment resistance.

Physical barrier

The biofilm matrix acts as a physical shield that prevents antibiotic molecules from reaching the bacteria inside. Studies have shown that antibiotics can be absorbed, degraded, or neutralized by the biofilm matrix before they reach their target. The concentration of antibiotic that actually contacts bacteria within a mature biofilm may be a fraction of what is present in the surrounding tissue.

Metabolic dormancy

Bacteria within the interior of a biofilm often enter a slow-growing or dormant state. Most antibiotics work by disrupting active metabolic processes — cell wall synthesis (beta-lactams), protein synthesis (tetracyclines), or DNA replication (fluoroquinolones). When bacteria are not actively dividing, these mechanisms of action are ineffective. The bacteria are not genetically resistant to the antibiotic; they are simply not doing the things the antibiotic is designed to disrupt.

Persister cells

Biofilms harbor a subpopulation of "persister" cells — dormant variants that are highly tolerant to antibiotics. These persisters can survive antibiotic treatment and repopulate the biofilm when treatment ends, leading to symptom relapse.

Immune evasion

The biofilm matrix also shields bacteria from the immune system. Immune cells such as neutrophils and macrophages cannot effectively penetrate the biofilm structure. Antibodies produced against Borrelia may not reach bacteria embedded within the matrix. This immune evasion perpetuates the infection and can contribute to ongoing immune dysregulation. How hyperthermia disrupts biofilms

This is where whole-body hyperthermia offers a fundamentally different approach to the problem of biofilm-protected Borrelia. Unlike antibiotics, which must penetrate the biofilm to reach bacteria, hyperthermia works through mechanisms that the biofilm cannot easily defend against.

Thermal disruption of biofilm structure

Elevated temperatures (41.6–42°C during whole-body hyperthermia) physically disrupt the biofilm matrix. The polysaccharide and protein components of the matrix are temperature-sensitive, and sustained elevated temperatures can weaken the structural integrity of the biofilm, creating openings that allow both immune cells and antimicrobial agents to access the bacteria within.

Enhanced antibiotic penetration

When hyperthermia weakens the biofilm matrix, antibiotics administered concurrently or immediately after hyperthermia can achieve higher concentrations within the biofilm than they could against an intact structure. This is why the combination of hyperthermia with targeted antibiotics is more effective than either modality alone.

Metabolic reactivation

Heat stimulates metabolic activity in dormant bacteria. As bacteria increase their metabolic rate in response to thermal stress, they become more susceptible to antibiotics that target active metabolic processes. The dormancy that protected them from antibiotics is disrupted by the same heat that is weakening their biofilm shield.

Immune system activation

Hyperthermia simultaneously stimulates the immune system, increasing the production and activity of immune cells. When this enhanced immune response is combined with a weakened biofilm barrier, the immune system can access and target bacteria that were previously hidden.

The Reisinger et al. study: 16x increased susceptibility

One of the most cited pieces of evidence supporting the hyperthermia-biofilm connection comes from research by Reisinger et al., published in 1996. This study examined the effect of elevated temperatures on bacterial susceptibility to antibiotics.

Key finding: The Reisinger et al. study demonstrated a 16-fold increase in antibiotic susceptibility of bacteria at elevated temperatures (41–42°C) compared to normal body temperature (37°C). This means that the same antibiotic, at the same concentration, was 16 times more effective at killing bacteria when combined with fever-range temperatures.

This finding has direct clinical relevance for Lyme disease treatment. It provides a scientific basis for the approach used at Klinik St. Georg: administering targeted antibiotics during or immediately after whole-body hyperthermia sessions, when bacterial susceptibility is at its highest and biofilm defenses are at their weakest.

The 16-fold improvement is particularly significant when considered alongside the fact that biofilm-protected bacteria can be up to 1,000 times more resistant than planktonic bacteria. While a 16-fold improvement does not fully overcome a 1,000-fold resistance factor, it represents a substantial shift in the therapeutic equation — particularly when combined with the physical disruption of biofilm structure and immune system activation that hyperthermia also provides.

Current research status

The science of Borrelia biofilms is an active and evolving field of research. While the clinical implications are significant, it is important to present the current state of knowledge accurately.

What has been established

  • Borrelia burgdorferi can form biofilms in vitro (laboratory settings)
  • Biofilm-like structures have been identified in human tissue from Lyme disease patients
  • Bacteria within Borrelia biofilms are significantly more resistant to standard antibiotics than planktonic forms
  • Elevated temperatures increase bacterial susceptibility to antibiotics
  • Biofilm formation may explain treatment failure and symptom relapse in a subset of Lyme disease patients

What is still being investigated

  • The precise conditions under which Borrelia forms biofilms in the human body
  • The extent to which biofilm formation contributes to chronic Lyme disease relative to other mechanisms (immune dysregulation, tissue damage, persister cells)
  • Optimal strategies for combining anti-biofilm approaches with antimicrobial therapy
  • The role of biofilm-disrupting agents as adjunctive therapies
