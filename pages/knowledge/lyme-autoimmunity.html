Molecular mimicry: when similarity becomes a weapon

Molecular mimicry is a well-established immunological phenomenon in which structural similarities between a pathogen's proteins and the host's own tissue proteins cause the immune system to attack both. Borrelia burgdorferi is particularly effective at triggering this process.

The outer surface proteins of Borrelia share structural homology with several human tissue proteins, including components found in nerve myelin, cardiac tissue, synovial membranes (joint lining), and thyroid tissue. When the immune system mounts a response against these bacterial proteins, the antibodies and immune cells it produces can cross-react with the structurally similar human proteins, leading to autoimmune tissue damage.

This mechanism explains a clinical pattern that puzzles many patients and physicians: why symptoms sometimes persist or even worsen after the bacteria have been significantly reduced by antibiotic treatment. The infection may have initiated the autoimmune process, but once established, the autoimmune response can become self-sustaining — independent of the original infectious trigger.

How the cascade begins

  1. Borrelia infects the host and the immune system correctly identifies bacterial proteins as foreign
  2. Antibodies and T-cells are produced targeting specific Borrelia surface proteins
  3. Some of these immune responses cross-react with structurally similar human tissue proteins
  4. Tissue damage occurs as the immune system attacks both foreign and self-targets
  5. Damaged tissue releases more self-proteins, further stimulating the autoimmune response
  6. A self-perpetuating cycle establishes that can continue even after the infection is treated

Autoimmune conditions associated with Lyme disease

Autoimmune arthritis (Lyme arthritis)

The most well-documented autoimmune complication of Lyme disease is antibiotic-refractory Lyme arthritis. Approximately 10–20% of patients with Lyme arthritis do not respond to antibiotic therapy. Research has demonstrated that in many of these cases, the ongoing joint inflammation is driven by autoimmune responses — particularly autoantibodies against endothelial cell growth factor and other synovial proteins — rather than by persistent infection. The arthritis is autoimmune in nature, triggered but no longer sustained by active Borrelia.

MS-like presentations

Neuroborreliosis can produce white matter lesions, demyelination, and neurological symptoms that closely mimic multiple sclerosis. Some patients initially diagnosed with MS are later found to have Lyme disease, and the question of whether Borrelia can trigger a genuine autoimmune demyelinating process — distinct from direct infectious damage — is an active area of research. The overlap between neuroborreliosis and MS is clinically significant enough that Lyme testing is recommended in MS workups, particularly in endemic areas.

Thyroid dysfunction

Autoimmune thyroid conditions — both Hashimoto's thyroiditis (hypothyroidism) and Graves' disease (hyperthyroidism) — have been reported at elevated rates in Lyme patients. Molecular mimicry between Borrelia proteins and thyroid tissue antigens is one proposed mechanism. Patients who develop unexplained thyroid dysfunction after Lyme disease should have both conditions evaluated and treated.

Other associations

Case reports and clinical observations have linked Lyme disease to other autoimmune phenomena including autoimmune neuropathies (Guillain-Barré-like presentations), autoimmune carditis, antiphospholipid syndrome, and various autoantibody-positive states. While causation is difficult to establish definitively in individual cases, the pattern across the literature supports a meaningful association between Borrelia infection and autoimmune triggering.

The post-infectious autoimmune hypothesis

The "post-infectious autoimmune" hypothesis attempts to bridge the divide in the Lyme disease debate. It proposes that many patients with persistent symptoms after Lyme treatment are experiencing an autoimmune process that was initiated by the infection but has become self-sustaining.

This hypothesis is clinically important because it suggests that effective treatment may require more than antibiotics alone. If the problem is a self-perpetuating autoimmune process, then:

  • Additional antibiotics may be insufficient if the autoimmune component is dominant
  • Immune-modulating therapies may be necessary to interrupt the autoimmune cycle
  • The infection and the autoimmune process may need to be addressed simultaneously
  • Some degree of immune system "reset" may be needed in severe cases

Clinical nuance: In practice, the distinction between persistent infection and post-infectious autoimmunity is rarely absolute. Many patients likely have elements of both: residual low-level infection driving ongoing immune stimulation alongside an established autoimmune process that has taken on a life of its own. Effective treatment must consider both possibilities rather than committing dogmatically to one explanation.

Why some patients do not improve with antibiotics alone

The autoimmune dimension of Lyme disease explains one of the most frustrating clinical scenarios: patients who receive adequate antibiotic treatment, who may even have evidence of reduced or eliminated bacterial burden, but who continue to suffer debilitating symptoms.

If the immune system has been reprogrammed by molecular mimicry to attack the patient's own tissues, killing the bacteria that initiated this process does not automatically stop it. The autoimmune "loop" has become independent of its trigger. This is analogous to other post-infectious autoimmune conditions — rheumatic heart disease, for example, where streptococcal infection triggers autoimmune cardiac damage that persists long after the strep is eradicated.

For these patients, treatment must evolve beyond an exclusively antimicrobial approach to include strategies that modulate or interrupt the autoimmune process itself.

Immune modulation and apheresis

H.E.L.P. apheresis

Heparin-induced Extracorporeal LDL Precipitation (H.E.L.P.) apheresis is a blood filtration procedure that can remove circulating immune complexes, autoantibodies, pro-inflammatory lipoproteins, and inflammatory mediators from the bloodstream. In the context of Lyme-triggered autoimmunity, apheresis serves a dual purpose: it reduces the circulating autoantibody burden and removes the inflammatory signals that perpetuate the autoimmune response. Patients often report noticeable improvement in symptoms after apheresis sessions, particularly in neurological and joint-related complaints.

Immunomodulatory strategies

Unlike crude immunosuppression — which carries risks of infection susceptibility and other complications — immunomodulation aims to recalibrate the immune response rather than suppress it wholesale. This may include targeted cytokine modulation, regulatory T-cell support, and anti-inflammatory interventions designed to break the autoimmune cycle without compromising the patient's ability to fight infection.

Hyperthermia as immune reset

Whole-body hyperthermia has immunomodulatory effects beyond its direct antimicrobial action. Controlled fever-range hyperthermia can influence cytokine profiles, stimulate heat shock protein responses, and modulate immune cell behavior in ways that may help interrupt dysregulated autoimmune patterns. This dual role — antimicrobial and immunomodulatory — makes hyperthermia particularly valuable in cases where persistent infection and autoimmunity coexist.

Klinik St. Georg's approach to Lyme-triggered autoimmunity

Our approach recognizes that Lyme-triggered autoimmunity is rarely a purely post-infectious phenomenon. Most patients present with a complex interplay of residual infection, immune dysregulation, and autoimmune tissue damage. Our treatment protocol therefore addresses all three dimensions:

  1. Address residual infection: Whole-body hyperthermia combined with targeted antibiotics aims to reduce any remaining bacterial burden that may be driving ongoing immune stimulation
  2. Reduce autoimmune burden: H.E.L.P. apheresis removes circulating autoantibodies and inflammatory mediators, providing immediate relief and interrupting the autoimmune cycle
  3. Modulate immune function: Immunomodulatory strategies support a return to balanced immune function rather than continued self-attack
  4. Support tissue recovery: Nutritional, anti-inflammatory, and regenerative support helps damaged tissues heal once the destructive processes have been interrupted
We do not believe that the infection vs. autoimmunity debate should determine patient care. Both processes can coexist, and effective treatment addresses both. The patient's response to treatment — not theoretical allegiance — should guide clinical decisions.
