Why the Borrelia species matters

Lyme disease is not caused by a single bacterium. It is caused by a group of closely related species within the Borrelia burgdorferi sensu lato complex. At least 21 species have been identified in this complex, of which at least 5 are confirmed to cause human disease.

This matters far more than most patients realize. The species you are infected with significantly influences which symptoms you develop, whether standard tests can detect your infection, and which treatment approach is most appropriate.

For international patients traveling to Germany for treatment, understanding species differences is particularly important. A patient from the United States is likely infected with a different species than a patient from central Europe, and these infections can present and respond differently.

Borrelia burgdorferi sensu stricto

B. burgdorferi sensu stricto (in the narrow sense) is the primary species causing Lyme disease in North America and is also present in parts of Europe. It was the first Lyme disease-causing species identified, by Willy Burgdorfer in 1982.

Geographic distribution

Predominant in the northeastern and upper midwestern United States, southeastern Canada, and present in parts of western and central Europe. In North America, it is essentially the only Borrelia species causing Lyme disease. In Europe, it coexists with other species.

Clinical signature

B. burgdorferi sensu stricto has a particular affinity for joints. The hallmark late manifestation in North America is Lyme arthritis — a recurrent or persistent inflammatory arthritis, most commonly affecting the knee. Other manifestations include the classic erythema migrans rash, neurological involvement, and cardiac disease, but the arthritic presentation is what most distinguishes this species clinically.

Testing considerations

Standard two-tier testing (ELISA + Western blot) was developed primarily against B. burgdorferi sensu stricto antigens. This means that testing sensitivity is generally best for this species, though significant gaps remain, particularly in early infection.

Borrelia afzelii

B. afzelii is one of the most common Borrelia species in Europe and Asia. It is responsible for a substantial proportion of Lyme disease cases across central and northern Europe.

Geographic distribution

Found across Europe from Portugal to Russia and into parts of East Asia. It is particularly prevalent in central Europe, including Germany, Austria, Switzerland, and Scandinavia. Patients infected in these regions have a high probability of carrying B. afzelii.

Clinical signature

B. afzelii has a strong affinity for the skin. Its hallmark manifestation is acrodermatitis chronica atrophicans (ACA) — a slowly progressive skin condition that causes the skin, typically on the extremities, to become thin, discolored, and atrophic over months to years. ACA is virtually pathognomonic for B. afzelii infection and is rarely if ever seen in North American Lyme disease. B. afzelii also causes erythema migrans, which may appear as a more uniform redness without a classic bull's-eye pattern.

Testing considerations

Standard tests developed against North American B. burgdorferi strains may have reduced sensitivity for B. afzelii. European laboratories that use locally relevant antigens generally achieve better detection rates. This is one reason why patients diagnosed and tested in the United States may receive negative results despite having a European-acquired Borrelia infection.

Borrelia garinii

B. garinii is the other major European Borrelia species and is clinically significant because of its pronounced affinity for the nervous system.

Geographic distribution

Widely distributed across Europe and Asia. It is particularly common in central and eastern Europe. Together with B. afzelii, it accounts for the majority of European Lyme disease cases.

Clinical signature

B. garinii is the primary cause of Lyme neuroborreliosis in Europe. It has a strong tropism for neural tissue, meaning it preferentially invades the nervous system. Clinical manifestations include meningitis, cranial neuritis (especially facial palsy), radiculopathy (Bannwarth syndrome with severe radiating pain), and encephalomyelitis. For a detailed exploration of neurological Lyme disease, see our guide on Lyme disease and the brain.

This neurotropism helps explain why neuroborreliosis is significantly more common in Europe than in North America — not because European patients are more susceptible, but because the predominant European Borrelia species is more neuroinvasive.

Testing considerations

As with B. afzelii, tests calibrated to North American strains may miss B. garinii infections. Specialized European laboratories offer broader antigen panels that improve detection of this species.

Borrelia miyamotoi

B. miyamotoi is a more recently recognized Borrelia species that causes a distinct illness. Unlike the other Borrelia species discussed above, it belongs to the relapsing fever group rather than the Lyme disease group.

Geographic distribution

Found in both North America and Eurasia, transmitted by the same tick species that carry Lyme-causing Borrelia. Its geographic range largely overlaps with that of Lyme disease, and co-infection is possible.

Clinical signature

B. miyamotoi causes a relapsing fever-like illness with high fevers that may recur over days to weeks, severe headache, myalgia, and fatigue. Unlike classic Lyme disease, it does not typically cause erythema migrans. In immunocompromised patients, it can cause meningoencephalitis. The relapsing nature of symptoms can be confusing and may delay diagnosis.

Testing considerations

Standard Lyme disease tests do not detect B. miyamotoi. Specific PCR or serological tests are required. Because these are not routinely ordered, B. miyamotoi infection is likely significantly underdiagnosed.

What this means for international patients

For patients traveling from North America, the UK, Australia, or other regions to seek treatment in Germany, understanding species differences has practical implications.

Your test results may need reinterpretation

If you were tested in North America using standard two-tier testing, your results were optimized for detecting B. burgdorferi sensu stricto. If you were actually infected with a European species (through travel, for example), or even with certain strains of B. burgdorferi, your tests may have produced false negatives. At Klinik St. Georg, we use or refer to laboratories with broad-spectrum Borrelia testing panels. See our guide on Lyme testing and diagnosis.

Your symptom profile provides diagnostic clues

A predominantly neurological presentation in a patient with European exposure strongly suggests B. garinii. A primarily arthritic presentation in a North American patient points to B. burgdorferi sensu stricto. These clinical patterns, combined with travel and exposure history, guide diagnostic and treatment decisions.

Treatment principles are universal

While species influence presentation and diagnosis, the core treatment principles at Klinik St. Georg apply across all pathogenic Borrelia species. Whole-body hyperthermia targets the thermal vulnerability of Borrelia spirochetes regardless of species. Combination antimicrobial protocols are designed to address different bacterial forms (spirochetes, round bodies, biofilms) that all pathogenic Borrelia species can adopt.

European advantage: Because Klinik St. Georg is located in Germany — where multiple Borrelia species circulate — our clinical experience encompasses the full range of Lyme disease presentations. We routinely treat patients infected with B. afzelii, B. garinii, and B. burgdorferi sensu stricto, giving us a broader clinical perspective than centers that encounter only one species.

What to do next

  1. Consider your exposure history. Where were you when you were likely bitten? This helps determine which Borrelia species are most probable.
  2. Review your test results. Were your tests calibrated for the species you are most likely to have? If not, consider specialized testing.
  3. Match symptoms to species. Understanding which species causes which symptoms can help explain your clinical picture and guide further evaluation.
  4. Reach out to us. If you would like a physician at Klinik St. Georg to review your case, you can share your story here.
Lyme disease is not one disease. It is a family of infections with distinct characteristics. Knowing which member of that family you are facing is the first step toward effective treatment.
