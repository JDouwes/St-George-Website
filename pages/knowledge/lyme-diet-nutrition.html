Lyme disease diet and nutrition

Reviewed by Julian Douwes M.D., Chief Medical Officer, Klinik St. Georg

Diet alone does not treat Lyme disease. But what you eat directly influences inflammation, immune function, gut health, and energy, all of which are central to how well your body responds to treatment and recovers afterward. Sound nutritional choices can meaningfully support your progress.

At a glance

Core principle: An anti-inflammatory diet reduces the burden on your immune system and supports the body's ability to fight infection and repair tissue damage caused by chronic Lyme disease.

Emphasize: Omega-3 fatty acids, colorful vegetables and berries, quality protein, healthy fats, and anti-inflammatory spices like turmeric and ginger.

Minimize: Refined sugar, alcohol, highly processed foods, and for sensitive patients, gluten and dairy. These can increase inflammation and impair immune function.

Anti-inflammatory diet principles

Chronic Lyme disease is, at its core, a disease of chronic inflammation. The immune system's prolonged response to Borrelia infection generates inflammatory cytokines that drive many of the symptoms patients experience: brain fog, fatigue, joint pain, and nerve pain. Anything that adds to the inflammatory burden makes symptoms worse. Anything that reduces it creates better conditions for treatment and recovery.

An anti-inflammatory diet is not a Lyme-specific diet. It is a well-established dietary approach supported by research in autoimmune disease, cardiovascular health, and chronic inflammatory conditions. For Lyme patients, the principles are the same but the stakes are higher: your body is already fighting on multiple fronts, and it needs every advantage you can give it.

Foods to emphasize

Omega-3 fatty acids are among the most potent dietary anti-inflammatories available. Wild-caught fatty fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel, anchovies), walnuts, flaxseeds, and chia seeds provide omega-3s that directly modulate the inflammatory response. Aim for fatty fish at least two to three times per week, or supplement with a high-quality fish oil.

Cruciferous vegetables such as broccoli, cauliflower, Brussels sprouts, kale, and cabbage contain sulforaphane and other compounds that support the body's detoxification pathways, particularly in the liver. This is especially important during treatment, when bacterial die-off increases the toxic load on the body.

Berries (blueberries, blackberries, raspberries, strawberries) are rich in anthocyanins and other polyphenols with documented anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties. They also support gut microbiome diversity.

Turmeric and ginger have well-established anti-inflammatory properties. Curcumin, the active compound in turmeric, modulates multiple inflammatory pathways. Combining turmeric with black pepper increases its bioavailability significantly.

Quality protein from pasture-raised poultry, wild fish, eggs, and legumes supports immune cell production and tissue repair. Chronic Lyme patients are often in a catabolic state, and adequate protein intake helps counter muscle wasting and supports recovery.

Healthy fats from extra-virgin olive oil, avocados, nuts, and seeds provide anti-inflammatory monounsaturated and polyunsaturated fatty acids, as well as fat-soluble vitamins that support immune function.

Foods to minimize or avoid

Refined sugar and high-glycemic carbohydrates trigger rapid insulin spikes and promote systemic inflammation. Sugar also feeds opportunistic organisms in the gut, which can compound the microbiome disruption many Lyme patients already experience from antibiotic use. Minimize added sugar, white flour, sugary beverages, and processed snacks.

Alcohol stresses the liver, impairs immune function, disrupts sleep, and increases inflammation. During active Lyme treatment, alcohol should be eliminated entirely. During recovery, moderation is essential.

Processed foods containing artificial additives, preservatives, seed oils, and trans fats contribute to inflammation and offer little nutritional value. Prioritize whole, unprocessed foods wherever possible.

Gluten is a consideration for patients who are sensitive or have documented intestinal permeability (commonly discussed as "leaky gut"). Not all Lyme patients need to eliminate gluten, but those who notice increased symptoms after consuming it may benefit from a trial elimination period. The connection between chronic infection, intestinal permeability, and gluten sensitivity is an area of active clinical discussion.

Dairy can be inflammatory for some individuals, particularly conventional dairy products. Patients who tolerate it well need not eliminate dairy entirely, but those with digestive issues or increased inflammation may benefit from reducing consumption or choosing fermented dairy (yogurt, kefir) over milk and cheese.

Supplements commonly used by Lyme patients

Supplements are not a substitute for a balanced diet, but certain nutrients are difficult to obtain in adequate quantities from food alone, particularly when the body is under the stress of chronic infection.

Probiotics are particularly important for patients who are taking or have recently taken antibiotics. Antibiotic therapy disrupts the gut microbiome, and probiotic supplementation helps restore microbial diversity. Look for multi-strain formulations containing Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium species, with a potency of at least 20 billion CFU. Take probiotics at least two hours apart from antibiotic doses.

Omega-3 fish oil supplements provide concentrated anti-inflammatory EPA and DHA. A typical therapeutic dose ranges from 2,000 to 4,000 mg of combined EPA and DHA daily. Choose a product tested for heavy metals and oxidation.

Vitamin D is essential for immune function and is commonly deficient in chronic Lyme patients. Testing vitamin D levels and supplementing to maintain optimal levels (typically 40–60 ng/mL) is a basic but important measure.

Magnesium supports muscle and nerve function, both commonly affected in Lyme disease. It also supports sleep quality and reduces muscle cramping. Magnesium glycinate or magnesium threonate are generally better tolerated than magnesium oxide.

Curcumin (the active compound in turmeric) is available in concentrated supplement form with enhanced bioavailability formulations. It offers anti-inflammatory support beyond what culinary turmeric can provide.

Nutritional support during hyperthermia treatment

Patients undergoing intensive treatment at Klinik St. Georg receive specific nutritional guidance tailored to the treatment protocol.

Before treatment: Adequate hydration and electrolyte balance are essential in the days before hyperthermia sessions. Patients are advised to eat a clean, anti-inflammatory diet, ensure adequate protein intake, and begin or continue probiotic supplementation.

During the treatment period: Light, easily digestible meals are recommended on treatment days. Heavy meals should be avoided before hyperthermia sessions. IV micronutrient support (vitamin C, glutathione, magnesium) supplements dietary intake during the intensive treatment phase.

Herxheimer management: When bacterial die-off occurs during treatment, the body's detoxification pathways are under significant strain. Nutritional support during this phase emphasizes hydration, electrolyte replenishment, glutathione-supporting foods (cruciferous vegetables, sulfur-rich foods), and easily absorbed nutrients.

Post-treatment nutritional recovery

After completing intensive treatment, nutritional focus shifts to rebuilding and repair:

  • Gut restoration: Probiotic-rich fermented foods (sauerkraut, kimchi, kefir, kombucha) alongside probiotic supplements help rebuild microbiome diversity disrupted by antibiotic therapy.
  • Tissue repair: Adequate protein, vitamin C, zinc, and collagen-supporting nutrients help the body repair tissue damage from chronic infection.
  • Energy recovery: B-vitamins, CoQ10, and iron (if deficient) support mitochondrial energy production as the body recovers from the metabolic demands of chronic illness and intensive treatment.
  • Ongoing anti-inflammatory diet: The dietary principles outlined above should be maintained long-term, not just during the treatment period. Chronic Lyme patients benefit from sustained attention to dietary quality.
Medical disclaimer: This page provides general educational information about nutrition in the context of Lyme disease. It is not medical advice or a prescribed diet plan. Nutritional needs vary by individual. Consult a qualified healthcare provider or registered dietitian before making significant dietary changes or beginning supplementation, particularly if you are taking medications.
