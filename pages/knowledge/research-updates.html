Lyme disease research updates

Reviewed by Julian Douwes M.D., Chief Medical Officer, Klinik St. Georg

The science of Lyme disease is evolving. This page highlights key research publications that inform our understanding of Borrelia persistence, the thermolability of Borrelia, hyperthermia for infectious disease, and post-treatment Lyme syndrome. This is a living document, last updated April 2026.

At a glance

Borrelia persistence: Multiple peer-reviewed studies have demonstrated that Borrelia burgdorferi can persist in tissue after standard antibiotic treatment in animal models, challenging the assumption that standard therapy always eradicates the infection.

Thermolability: The foundational Reisinger et al. (1996) study demonstrated that Borrelia is thermolabile, losing viability at temperatures above 41.6 degrees Celsius, providing the scientific basis for hyperthermia treatment.

Where the field is heading: Increasing recognition of treatment-resistant Lyme, growing research into alternative and adjunctive therapies, and a gradual shift toward acknowledging the limitations of antibiotic-only approaches.

Thermolability of Borrelia: Reisinger et al. 1996

Published research

The study that provides the scientific foundation for hyperthermia treatment of Lyme disease was published by Reisinger et al. in 1996. The researchers demonstrated that Borrelia burgdorferi is thermolabile, meaning it loses structural integrity and viability when exposed to temperatures above its thermal tolerance threshold.

Specifically, the study showed that Borrelia cultures exposed to temperatures of 41.6 degrees Celsius and above for sustained periods exhibited significant loss of motility and viability. At 42 degrees Celsius, the bacteria showed rapid structural degradation.

Why this matters clinically: Whole-body hyperthermia can elevate core body temperature to 41.6–42 degrees Celsius under controlled medical conditions. If Borrelia becomes structurally compromised at these temperatures, hyperthermia offers a mechanism of action fundamentally different from antibiotics: direct thermal damage to the organism itself, rather than biochemical interference with its metabolism.

This study remains the foundational reference for hyperthermia-based Lyme treatment and is the starting point for the clinical rationale used at Klinik St. Georg.

Borrelia persistence research

Published research

One of the most important developments in Lyme research over the past two decades has been the growing body of evidence that Borrelia burgdorferi can persist in tissue despite antibiotic treatment. This directly challenges the long-held position that standard antibiotic courses reliably eradicate the infection.

Embers et al. (2012): In a landmark study published in PLOS ONE, researchers at Tulane University demonstrated that Borrelia burgdorferi persisted in tissues of non-human primates (rhesus macaques) after aggressive antibiotic treatment with 28 days of doxycycline. The surviving organisms were found in multiple organs and tissues, including the brain, heart, bladder, and joints. While the clinical significance of these persistent organisms remains debated, the study provided compelling evidence that standard antibiotic treatment does not always achieve sterilizing cure.

Feng et al. (2014, 2015): Researchers at Johns Hopkins identified that Borrelia burgdorferi forms drug-tolerant persister cells, similar to the persister phenomenon observed in other bacterial species. These persister cells survive antibiotic exposure not through genetic resistance but through metabolic dormancy, a state in which the bacteria are alive but not actively dividing, making them invisible to antibiotics that target active metabolic processes. Feng's group also identified drug combinations that showed greater activity against these persister forms in vitro.

Caskey and Embers (2015): Additional primate studies confirmed that persistent Borrelia retained the ability to infect ticks feeding on treated animals, suggesting that post-treatment persister organisms are not merely non-functional debris but may retain biological activity.

Sharma et al. (2015): Research demonstrated that Borrelia forms biofilms in vitro and potentially in vivo. Biofilm communities are inherently resistant to both antibiotics and immune clearance, providing another mechanism by which the organism may survive treatment.

Hyperthermia research for infectious disease

Clinical experience

While hyperthermia is best known in oncology, where it has an extensive evidence base for enhancing radiation and chemotherapy, its application to infectious disease is an emerging area of research.

Rationale: The use of fever as a defense mechanism against infection is one of the oldest strategies in biology. The human body elevates its temperature during infection precisely because elevated temperature impairs many pathogens while enhancing immune cell function. Whole-body hyperthermia takes this principle to its therapeutic conclusion by achieving temperatures that exceed what the body can generate through natural fever.

Historical precedent: Before the antibiotic era, fever therapy (pyrotherapy) was used to treat syphilis, another spirochetal infection caused by Treponema pallidum. Julius Wagner-Jauregg received the Nobel Prize in Medicine in 1927 for this work. The parallel between Treponema (syphilis) and Borrelia (Lyme) is noteworthy: both are spirochetes, both are thermolabile, and both cause multi-system chronic disease when untreated.

Current research gaps: Large-scale, randomized controlled trials of hyperthermia specifically for Lyme disease have not yet been conducted. The clinical evidence base consists of the thermolability data (Reisinger et al.), the historical precedent of pyrotherapy for spirochetal infections, clinical case series from treatment centers including Klinik St. Georg, and the broader hyperthermia literature from oncology demonstrating safety and immune modulation.

We acknowledge this gap transparently. The absence of large RCTs does not mean the treatment is ineffective, but it does mean the evidence is not yet at the level that would satisfy conventional guideline committees. This is a problem of research funding and interest, not of biological plausibility.

Post-treatment Lyme disease syndrome

Published research

Post-Treatment Lyme Disease Syndrome (PTLDS) is the term used in mainstream medical literature for the constellation of symptoms that persist in a subset of patients after standard antibiotic treatment for Lyme disease.

Prevalence: Studies estimate that 10–20% of patients treated for acute Lyme disease with standard antibiotic regimens develop PTLDS, experiencing fatigue, musculoskeletal pain, and cognitive difficulties for six months or longer after treatment.

NIH-sponsored trials: Several randomized controlled trials (Klempner et al. 2001, Krupp et al. 2003, Fallon et al. 2008) examined whether additional antibiotic courses improve PTLDS symptoms. Results were mixed: some showed short-term improvement (particularly in fatigue) but no sustained benefit, and the trials raised concerns about risks of prolonged antibiotic therapy. These trials are frequently cited as evidence against additional antibiotics for PTLDS, though critics argue the study designs, treatment durations, and patient selection were inadequate.

Biomarker research: Recent studies have begun identifying potential biomarkers for PTLDS, including elevated inflammatory cytokines (CCL19, IL-23), altered T-cell populations, and metabolic signatures that distinguish PTLDS patients from recovered patients and healthy controls. These findings support the biological reality of PTLDS and may eventually enable objective diagnosis and treatment monitoring.

Neuroimaging studies: SPECT and PET brain imaging studies in PTLDS patients have demonstrated patterns of reduced cerebral blood flow and neuroinflammation, correlating with the cognitive symptoms patients report. These findings provide objective evidence that PTLDS involves measurable changes in brain function, not merely subjective symptom reporting.

Where the field is heading

Lyme disease research is at an inflection point. Several trends are reshaping the landscape:

Increasing recognition of treatment limitations. The medical community is gradually acknowledging that standard antibiotic protocols do not adequately serve all Lyme patients. Major research institutions including Johns Hopkins, Columbia, and Tulane are conducting research on Borrelia persistence, persister cells, and novel treatment approaches.

Novel antimicrobial strategies. Research into drug combinations, disulfiram, daptomycin, and other agents that may be more effective against persister forms of Borrelia is ongoing. The Feng et al. work at Johns Hopkins on identifying drug combinations effective against stationary-phase Borrelia is particularly promising.

Diagnostic improvements. Next-generation diagnostic approaches, including direct detection methods, metabolomics-based testing, and improved serological assays, may eventually solve the diagnostic limitations that currently delay treatment for many patients.

Integrative and multimodal approaches. There is growing interest in treatment strategies that combine multiple mechanisms of action rather than relying on antibiotics alone. Hyperthermia, immunotherapy, and biotoxin management are areas of increasing clinical and research attention.

Patient advocacy driving research. Organizations such as the Global Lyme Alliance, Bay Area Lyme Foundation, and LymeDisease.org are funding research that government agencies have historically underfunded. Patient advocacy has been instrumental in keeping Lyme disease research alive and pushing for recognition of chronic Lyme as a legitimate medical condition requiring novel treatment approaches.
