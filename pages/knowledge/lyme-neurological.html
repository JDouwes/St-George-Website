What is neuroborreliosis?

Neuroborreliosis is the involvement of the nervous system in Lyme disease. It occurs when Borrelia spirochetes disseminate from the initial site of infection and cross the blood-brain barrier to enter the central nervous system. In Europe, neurological involvement is particularly common because Borrelia garinii — the predominant European species — has a strong predilection for nervous tissue. Learn more about species differences in our guide on Borrelia species.

Neuroborreliosis is estimated to occur in 10–15% of untreated Lyme disease cases and is the second most common manifestation of disseminated Lyme disease in Europe, after skin involvement. However, the true incidence may be significantly higher, as neurological symptoms can be subtle and are frequently attributed to other conditions.

Borrelia crosses the blood-brain barrier through multiple mechanisms. The spirochetes can traverse endothelial cells directly, exploit gaps between cells during inflammation, and travel along peripheral nerves into the CNS. Once inside, they can persist in the cerebrospinal fluid, meninges, nerve roots, and brain parenchyma, where antibiotic concentrations are often insufficient to eliminate them.

Neurological symptoms of Lyme disease

The neurological manifestations of Lyme disease range from mild to devastating. They can appear weeks to months after the initial infection and, in chronic cases, may persist for years.

Cognitive dysfunction ("brain fog")

Perhaps the most commonly reported neurological symptom among Lyme patients. It encompasses difficulty concentrating, impaired short-term memory, word-finding problems, slowed processing speed, poor executive function, and difficulty multitasking. Patients frequently describe a sense that their thinking is clouded or sluggish — a marked contrast to their cognitive baseline before illness.

Peripheral neuropathy

Damage to peripheral nerves causes numbness, tingling, burning sensations, shooting pains, and weakness — most commonly in the hands and feet but potentially affecting any part of the body. Small fiber neuropathy, which affects the smallest sensory nerves, is particularly common in Lyme disease and can cause widespread burning pain and autonomic dysfunction.

Cranial nerve palsies

Facial palsy (Bell's palsy) is one of the hallmark neurological presentations of Lyme disease. It can be unilateral or bilateral. Other cranial nerves can also be affected, causing double vision, hearing changes, vertigo, or difficulty swallowing.

Radiculopathy

Inflammation of nerve roots — a condition known as Bannwarth syndrome when it occurs in the context of Lyme disease — causes severe radiating pain, often worse at night. This can mimic disc herniation or other structural spinal problems and is frequently misdiagnosed.

Meningitis and encephalitis

Borrelia can cause inflammation of the meninges (meningitis) and, in more severe cases, of the brain tissue itself (encephalitis). Symptoms include severe headache, neck stiffness, light sensitivity, confusion, and altered mental status. Lyme meningitis in particular tends to present more subtly than bacterial meningitis caused by other organisms.

Psychiatric symptoms

Depression, anxiety, panic attacks, irritability, depersonalization, and mood instability are common in neurological Lyme disease. These can be direct effects of neuroinflammation on brain circuitry rather than psychological responses to chronic illness, though both mechanisms often coexist.

How Lyme disrupts brain networks

Emerging research in neuroimaging has revealed that Lyme disease does not simply cause random neurological damage. Instead, it appears to selectively disrupt specific brain networks that are critical for cognition, attention, and emotional regulation.

Default Mode Network (DMN)

The DMN is active during rest and self-referential thinking. Disruption of this network is associated with the difficulty concentrating, mental fatigue, and "foggy" thinking that Lyme patients describe. Neuroimaging studies have shown altered DMN connectivity in patients with post-treatment Lyme disease.

Salience Network

The salience network determines which stimuli deserve attention and helps switch between internal and external focus. Dysfunction in this network may explain the sensory hypersensitivity, difficulty filtering relevant information, and heightened startle responses common in neurological Lyme disease.

Central Executive Network (CEN)

The CEN supports working memory, decision-making, and problem-solving. Impairment of this network underlies the executive dysfunction, difficulty with multitasking, and impaired planning ability that many Lyme patients experience.

The neuroinflammation cascade

The neurological damage in Lyme disease is caused not only by the direct presence of bacteria but also by the inflammatory response they trigger. Understanding this cascade is essential for effective treatment.

Microglia activation

Microglia are the resident immune cells of the brain. When Borrelia enters the CNS, microglia become activated and release pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-1beta, IL-6). In acute infection, this response is protective. In chronic infection, sustained microglial activation becomes destructive — damaging neurons and disrupting the delicate neurochemical balance required for normal brain function.

Cytokine cascades

The inflammatory cytokines released by activated microglia and other immune cells create a self-amplifying inflammatory environment in the brain. These cytokines impair neurotransmitter function, reduce neuroplasticity, disrupt the blood-brain barrier further, and can directly damage myelin — the insulating sheath around nerve fibers. This helps explain why neurological symptoms can persist even after the bacterial burden has been reduced.

Oxidative stress

Chronic neuroinflammation generates excessive reactive oxygen species that cause oxidative damage to neurons. This oxidative stress contributes to neuronal dysfunction and death, and may accelerate neurodegenerative processes in vulnerable patients.

The diagnostic challenge

Diagnosing neurological Lyme disease is among the most difficult tasks in clinical medicine. Standard blood tests for Lyme disease may be negative despite active CNS infection, and neurological symptoms can mimic many other conditions.

Lumbar puncture (spinal tap)

Analysis of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) is considered the gold standard for diagnosing neuroborreliosis. Findings that support the diagnosis include elevated white blood cells (lymphocytic pleocytosis), elevated protein, intrathecal antibody production (comparing CSF and serum antibody levels), and positive Borrelia culture or PCR. However, CSF can be normal in chronic cases, particularly when infection has been partially treated.

MRI findings

Brain MRI may reveal white matter lesions that can resemble those seen in multiple sclerosis. Meningeal enhancement, cranial nerve enhancement, and nerve root enhancement may also be visible. However, MRI can be entirely normal in many patients with confirmed neuroborreliosis, particularly in chronic cases.

SPECT and PET imaging

Functional brain imaging with SPECT (single photon emission computed tomography) or PET (positron emission tomography) can reveal patterns of reduced blood flow or metabolic activity in the brain that are characteristic of neurological Lyme disease. These functional changes often correlate with cognitive symptoms and may be present even when structural MRI is normal. While not universally available, these tools can be valuable in complex diagnostic cases.

Neuropsychological testing

Formal cognitive testing can document and quantify the specific patterns of cognitive impairment associated with Lyme disease, distinguish them from depression and anxiety, and provide a baseline for monitoring treatment response.

Clinical assessment matters most: No single test can definitively confirm or exclude neurological Lyme disease. Diagnosis requires integrating clinical symptoms, exposure history, serological testing, CSF analysis when indicated, and neuroimaging into a comprehensive clinical picture. At Klinik St. Georg, our experience with thousands of neurological Lyme cases informs this integrative diagnostic approach.

Treating neurological Lyme disease

Effective treatment of neurological Lyme disease requires strategies that can penetrate the blood-brain barrier, address neuroinflammation, and support neurological recovery.

Targeted antimicrobial therapy

Standard treatment for neuroborreliosis involves IV antibiotics (typically ceftriaxone) that achieve adequate CNS concentrations. For chronic cases, combination protocols that address different bacterial forms may be necessary.

Whole-body hyperthermia

Hyperthermia has particular relevance for neurological Lyme disease because elevated core body temperature increases blood-brain barrier permeability to antimicrobials, enhances immune activity within the CNS, and directly damages spirochetes in tissues where antibiotics alone may not reach therapeutic concentrations.

Neurostimulation

Targeted neurostimulation can modulate the disrupted brain networks described above, helping to restore normal connectivity patterns and support cognitive recovery. This is used as an adjunctive therapy alongside antimicrobial treatment. Learn more on our neurostimulation page.

Neuroprotective support

Addressing neuroinflammation and oxidative stress through IV therapies, antioxidant support, and anti-inflammatory protocols is essential for protecting neurons during treatment and supporting long-term neurological recovery.

What to do if you have neurological symptoms

  1. Take your symptoms seriously. Brain fog, neuropathy, and cognitive changes are not normal aging or stress. If they began after a tick bite or alongside other Lyme symptoms, neurological involvement should be investigated.
  2. Seek specialized evaluation. Neurological Lyme disease requires clinicians who understand both infectious disease and neurology in the context of Borrelia infection.
  3. Consider advanced diagnostics. If standard testing has been negative but symptoms persist, specialized testing and functional neuroimaging may provide answers.
  4. Assess your symptoms. Our symptom assessment tool can help you evaluate whether your neurological symptoms may be consistent with Lyme disease.
  5. Reach out to us. If you would like a physician at Klinik St. Georg to review your case, you can share your story here.
