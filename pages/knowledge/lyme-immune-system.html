How Borrelia evades the immune system

Borrelia burgdorferi is one of the most sophisticated immune evasion organisms in nature. Over millions of years of co-evolution with mammalian hosts, it has developed an extraordinary array of strategies to survive and persist despite a fully functional immune system. Understanding these strategies explains why Lyme disease can become chronic and why simply "boosting" the immune system is not sufficient.

Antigenic variation

Borrelia possesses a remarkable ability to alter its outer surface proteins — the very molecules the immune system uses to identify and target the bacteria. Through a process of gene recombination, Borrelia can express different variants of its VlsE (Variable major protein-like sequence Expressed) surface antigen, effectively changing its appearance to the immune system. By the time the immune system has produced antibodies against one variant, the bacterium has already switched to a new one. This process has been compared to a fugitive constantly changing disguises.

Complement evasion

The complement system is one of the body's first lines of immune defense — a cascade of proteins that can directly kill bacteria and flag them for destruction by immune cells. Borrelia produces proteins (such as CRASPs — Complement Regulator-Acquiring Surface Proteins) that bind host complement regulators to its own surface, effectively co-opting the body's own protective mechanisms to shield itself from complement-mediated killing.

Immune cell manipulation

Borrelia can directly interfere with the function of immune cells. It has been shown to suppress the function of natural killer cells, inhibit the maturation and activation of dendritic cells (which are critical for initiating adaptive immune responses), and induce the production of anti-inflammatory cytokines that dampen the immune response. In effect, the bacteria can partially suppress the very immune response that should be eliminating them.

Tissue sequestration

Borrelia can penetrate deep into tissues where immune surveillance is limited. It invades collagen-rich structures (tendons, ligaments, joint capsules), crosses the blood-brain barrier into the central nervous system, and can even enter the interior of host cells. In these protected niches, it is shielded from circulating antibodies and immune cells. For more on CNS involvement, see our guide on Lyme disease and the brain.

Biofilm formation

By forming biofilms, Borrelia creates physical barriers that prevent immune cells from reaching and destroying the bacteria. The biofilm matrix also absorbs and neutralizes antibodies and complement proteins. Learn more in our guide on biofilms in Lyme disease.

Why this matters clinically: These evasion strategies mean that a negative antibody test does not necessarily mean the absence of infection. If Borrelia is actively suppressing antibody production or hiding from the immune system, standard antibody-based tests may fail to detect the infection even when it is present. This is one reason we rely on comprehensive clinical evaluation rather than test results alone.

Immune dysregulation in chronic Lyme

Perhaps more damaging than immune evasion itself is the immune dysregulation that Borrelia triggers. In chronic Lyme disease, the immune system often becomes both overactive in some pathways and underactive in others — a state of dysfunctional inflammation that perpetuates symptoms even when the bacterial load is low.

Th1/Th2 imbalance

The immune system has two major arms: Th1 (cell-mediated immunity, which fights intracellular pathogens) and Th2 (humoral immunity, which produces antibodies). In chronic Lyme disease, this balance is frequently disrupted. The Th1 response, which is critical for clearing Borrelia from tissues, may be suppressed, while the Th2 response may be inappropriately activated. This imbalance can perpetuate infection while simultaneously driving allergic-type inflammation and autoimmune tendencies.

Chronic inflammatory state

Borrelia triggers the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-1beta, IL-6, IL-8) that create a sustained inflammatory environment in affected tissues. This chronic inflammation causes tissue damage, pain, fatigue, and cognitive impairment — many of the symptoms that define the Lyme disease experience. Critically, this inflammation can become self-sustaining, continuing even after the bacteria that initiated it have been reduced or eliminated.

Exhausted immune response

Prolonged stimulation of the immune system by a persistent pathogen can lead to immune exhaustion — a state in which immune cells become less effective over time. T cells may become "exhausted," expressing inhibitory receptors and losing their ability to effectively fight the infection. This creates a vicious cycle: the immune system cannot clear the infection, and the persistent infection further exhausts the immune system.

Mast cell activation

Some patients with chronic Lyme disease develop mast cell activation syndrome (MCAS), in which mast cells (immune cells involved in allergic and inflammatory responses) become hyperreactive. This can cause a wide range of symptoms including flushing, hives, gastrointestinal distress, rapid heart rate, and neurological symptoms. MCAS may be triggered or exacerbated by chronic Borrelia infection and the associated immune dysregulation.

Autoimmune complications triggered by Borrelia

One of the most concerning consequences of Borrelia infection is the potential to trigger autoimmune responses — situations in which the immune system attacks the body's own tissues.

Molecular mimicry

Certain Borrelia proteins share structural similarities with human proteins. When the immune system produces antibodies against these Borrelia proteins, those antibodies may cross-react with similar human proteins, attacking healthy tissue. This process, known as molecular mimicry, has been implicated in Lyme arthritis (where antibodies cross-react with joint proteins) and potentially in neurological Lyme disease.

Autoimmune Lyme arthritis

A subset of patients with Lyme arthritis develop a treatment-resistant form in which joint inflammation persists even after successful elimination of the bacteria. This is believed to be driven by autoimmune mechanisms — the immune response initially directed against Borrelia becomes redirected against joint tissue. This form of Lyme arthritis requires immune-modulating treatment rather than further antibiotics.

Neurological autoimmunity

Borrelia infection of the nervous system can trigger autoimmune neurological damage. Antibodies generated against Borrelia antigens may cross-react with neural tissue, contributing to demyelination, neuropathy, and cognitive impairment that persists beyond the active infection. This mechanism may contribute to the neurological symptoms described in our guide on Lyme disease and the brain.

Other autoimmune associations

Clinical observation and emerging research suggest associations between Borrelia infection and a variety of autoimmune conditions, including thyroiditis, autoimmune neuropathies, and connective tissue disorders. While causation is difficult to establish definitively, the temporal relationship between Borrelia infection and autoimmune onset in individual patients is often striking.

Immune modulation as part of treatment

Because immune dysregulation is central to the chronic Lyme disease experience, effective treatment must address the immune system as well as the bacteria. At Klinik St. Georg, immune modulation is an integral component of our comprehensive treatment protocols.

The goal: balanced immune function

The objective is not simply to stimulate or suppress the immune system, but to restore balanced function. This means supporting the Th1 response needed to fight Borrelia, calming inappropriate inflammatory responses that drive symptoms, addressing autoimmune mechanisms where present, and restoring the function of exhausted immune cells.

Hyperthermia as immune therapy

Whole-body hyperthermia has profound immune-modulating effects beyond its direct antimicrobial action. Controlled fever therapy stimulates heat shock protein production (which enhances immune cell function), increases natural killer cell activity, promotes the release of immune-signaling molecules, and can help reset dysfunctional inflammatory patterns. This immune activation is one of the mechanisms that makes hyperthermia effective even in patients who have not responded to antibiotics alone.

Anti-inflammatory strategies

Reducing the chronic inflammatory burden through targeted anti-inflammatory protocols can break the cycle of inflammation-driven symptoms. This may include specific anti-inflammatory nutrients, dietary modifications, and pharmacological interventions tailored to each patient's inflammatory profile.

Microbiome support

The gut microbiome plays a critical role in immune regulation. Chronic Lyme disease and its treatment (particularly prolonged antibiotics) can significantly disrupt the microbiome, further contributing to immune dysregulation. Restoring a healthy microbiome through targeted probiotic therapy and nutritional support is an important component of immune recovery.

IV therapies and immune support at Klinik St. Georg

Intravenous (IV) therapies are a cornerstone of immune support in our treatment protocols. They allow precise delivery of therapeutic agents at concentrations that cannot be achieved through oral supplementation.

High-dose vitamin C

IV vitamin C at therapeutic doses has both antioxidant and pro-oxidant effects, depending on concentration. At higher concentrations, it generates hydrogen peroxide in tissues, which has antimicrobial activity while simultaneously supporting immune cell function. It also helps reduce the oxidative stress associated with chronic infection and inflammation.

Glutathione support

Glutathione is the body's primary intracellular antioxidant and plays a critical role in immune cell function and detoxification. Chronic Lyme disease depletes glutathione reserves, impairing both immune function and the body's ability to manage the oxidative stress of ongoing infection and inflammation. IV glutathione can help restore these depleted levels.

Micronutrient optimization

Zinc, selenium, vitamin D, and B-complex vitamins are all essential for proper immune function. Chronic infection often depletes these nutrients. IV delivery ensures optimal tissue levels and supports the recovery of immune competence.

Detoxification support

As bacteria are killed during treatment (whether by antibiotics, hyperthermia, or the immune system), bacterial debris and endotoxins can temporarily worsen symptoms — a phenomenon often called a Herxheimer reaction. IV support during and after treatment helps the body process and eliminate these toxins more efficiently, reducing this treatment-related symptom burden.

Individualized protocols: No two patients with chronic Lyme disease have identical immune profiles. At Klinik St. Georg, IV therapy and immune support protocols are tailored to each patient based on comprehensive laboratory assessment of immune function, inflammatory markers, micronutrient levels, and individual treatment response. There is no one-size-fits-all approach to immune restoration.

What to do next

  1. Understand your immune status. If you have chronic Lyme disease, comprehensive immune assessment can reveal the specific patterns of dysregulation driving your symptoms.
  2. Look beyond antibiotics. If antibiotics alone have not resolved your symptoms, immune dysregulation may be a significant contributing factor that needs to be addressed directly.
  3. Consider comprehensive treatment. Effective treatment of chronic Lyme disease addresses the infection, the immune dysregulation, and the resulting tissue damage simultaneously.
  4. Reach out to us. If you would like a physician at Klinik St. Georg to review your case and discuss immune assessment, you can share your story here.
Your immune system is not failing you. It is fighting an adversary that has had millions of years to learn how to evade it. The right treatment does not replace your immune system — it gives your immune system the advantage it needs to win.
