Why Lyme disease in children deserves special attention

Children are among the most frequently affected by Lyme disease. In endemic areas, the highest incidence rates occur in children aged 5–14 — precisely the age group that spends the most time playing outdoors in grass, woods, and fields where tick exposure is greatest.

Yet despite this high incidence, pediatric Lyme disease is frequently misdiagnosed or diagnosed late. Children often cannot articulate their symptoms as clearly as adults. The symptoms themselves may differ from textbook adult presentations. And many pediatricians, while excellent general practitioners, may not have specific training in recognizing the varied manifestations of Lyme disease beyond the classic bull's-eye rash.

At Klinik St. Georg, we have treated Lyme disease in patients across all age groups. We understand the particular urgency that parents feel when their child is ill, and we approach pediatric cases with the additional care and attention they require.

How Lyme disease presents differently in children

While children can experience any of the symptoms associated with adult Lyme disease, certain features are more common, more prominent, or present differently in pediatric patients.

The rash may look different

The erythema migrans rash in children may appear atypical. It may be smaller, less defined, or lack the classic bull's-eye pattern. In children with darker skin tones, the rash may be particularly difficult to identify. Parents and physicians should consider any expanding rash after possible tick exposure as suspicious, regardless of its exact appearance.

Joint symptoms predominate

Lyme arthritis is one of the most common manifestations of pediatric Lyme disease, particularly in North America. Children may present with a swollen, warm knee or other large joint. This is frequently misdiagnosed as juvenile idiopathic arthritis, septic arthritis, or "growing pains." The key differentiator is that Lyme arthritis often involves a single large joint and may be intermittent, with episodes lasting days to weeks.

Facial palsy

Facial nerve palsy (Bell's palsy) in children should always prompt investigation for Lyme disease, particularly in endemic areas. Lyme disease is among the most common causes of facial palsy in children, and bilateral facial palsy is especially suggestive of Lyme etiology.

Behavioral and cognitive changes

Children with Lyme disease frequently exhibit behavioral changes that may be attributed to other causes. These include increased irritability, mood swings, difficulty concentrating, declining academic performance, regression in previously mastered skills, and heightened anxiety. In younger children, these changes may be the primary presenting feature rather than physical complaints.

Fatigue and activity changes

A previously active child who becomes unusually tired, loses interest in sports or play, or complains frequently of feeling unwell should be evaluated. Children may not describe fatigue in the same terms adults use — they may simply refuse to participate, become clingy, or have more frequent meltdowns.

The impact on school and daily life

For school-age children with Lyme disease, the impact on education and social development can be significant and is often underappreciated.

Attention and concentration

Lyme-related cognitive impairment can cause difficulty concentrating during lessons, slower processing of information, difficulty following multi-step instructions, and trouble with reading comprehension. These symptoms are frequently misinterpreted as ADHD, learning disabilities, or lack of motivation.

Fatigue and attendance

Chronic fatigue may lead to increased absences, difficulty maintaining a full school day, falling behind in coursework, and social isolation from peers. The cyclical nature of Lyme symptoms — with good days and bad days — can make the pattern confusing for teachers who may interpret variable performance as inconsistent effort.

Physical limitations

Joint pain, headaches, and general malaise can prevent participation in physical education, recess, and extracurricular activities. The loss of these outlets can compound the emotional burden of illness.

Emotional impact

Children with undiagnosed or undertreated Lyme disease may develop anxiety about their health, frustration at being unable to keep up with peers, sadness about lost activities, and low self-esteem from academic struggles. These emotional responses are entirely normal but require recognition and support.

For parents navigating school: If your child has been diagnosed with Lyme disease, communicate openly with their school. Provide medical documentation, request accommodations if needed (modified schedules, extra time on assignments, rest breaks), and ensure teachers understand that symptoms may fluctuate. Your child is not being lazy — they are fighting an infection.

Diagnosis challenges in children

Diagnosing Lyme disease in children presents several unique challenges beyond the general testing limitations that affect all age groups.

Children may not report tick bites

Young children may not notice a tick attached to their body, particularly in areas they cannot easily see (scalp, back, groin). They may not recognize the significance of a rash. By the time symptoms develop, the initial tick exposure may be weeks or months in the past and easily forgotten.

Symptoms mimic common childhood conditions

Many Lyme symptoms overlap with common pediatric conditions: "growing pains" (joint pain), viral fatigue, behavioral issues, ADHD, anxiety disorders, and learning disabilities. Without a high index of suspicion, Lyme disease may not be considered in the differential diagnosis.

Testing limitations in children

The same limitations of standard two-tier testing apply to children. Children's immune systems may produce antibody responses that differ from adult patterns, potentially affecting test sensitivity. Specialized testing through laboratories experienced in pediatric Lyme disease may provide more accurate results.

When to suspect Lyme disease in a child

  • Unexplained joint swelling, especially in a single large joint
  • Facial palsy, unilateral or bilateral
  • New-onset headaches, especially with neck stiffness
  • Sudden behavioral changes or declining school performance
  • Unexplained fatigue that does not resolve with rest
  • Any expanding rash after outdoor activities in endemic areas
  • A constellation of symptoms that does not fit a single diagnosis

Treatment considerations for children

Treatment of pediatric Lyme disease follows similar principles to adult treatment but requires additional considerations for the developing child.

Standard antibiotic treatment

For early Lyme disease in children, oral antibiotics are the first-line treatment. Amoxicillin is typically used for children under 8 years old (doxycycline is generally avoided in younger children due to concerns about tooth discoloration, though recent evidence has softened this restriction for short courses). Doxycycline is the preferred agent for children 8 years and older. Duration is typically 14–21 days for early disease.

When standard treatment is insufficient

As with adults, some children do not respond adequately to standard antibiotic courses. In these cases, extended treatment, combination therapy, or advanced modalities may need to be considered. The decision to pursue advanced treatment in a child requires careful clinical judgment, weighing the risks of ongoing untreated disease against the demands of treatment.

Hyperthermia considerations

Whole-body hyperthermia treatment at Klinik St. Georg is performed under careful medical supervision. For pediatric patients, additional monitoring and age-appropriate protocols ensure safety. The decision to pursue hyperthermia for a child is made on a case-by-case basis after thorough evaluation, considering the severity and duration of symptoms, the child's overall health, and the response to prior treatments.

Supporting recovery

Children recovering from Lyme disease benefit from a gradual return to normal activities, nutritional support for immune function, adequate sleep (which may require temporarily adjusted schedules), emotional support and reassurance, and close monitoring for symptom recurrence.

Family support and advocacy

When a child has Lyme disease, the entire family is affected. Parents become advocates, caregivers, and often researchers, all while managing their own emotional response to their child's illness.

What parents can do

  • Educate yourself. Understanding Lyme disease empowers you to make informed decisions and communicate effectively with medical providers. Our knowledge base is a good starting point.
  • Document everything. Keep a detailed log of your child's symptoms, medications, and responses. Note patterns — what makes symptoms better or worse, time-of-day variations, and correlations with activity levels.
  • Seek specialized care. If your child is not improving with standard treatment, seek clinicians with specific pediatric Lyme experience. Do not accept dismissal of your child's symptoms.
  • Coordinate with school. Work proactively with teachers, school counselors, and administrators. Provide medical documentation and specific recommendations for classroom accommodations.
  • Support siblings. The siblings of a child with chronic illness often feel neglected or confused. Include them in age-appropriate conversations about what is happening and ensure they receive attention and support as well.
  • Take care of yourself. Parenting a sick child is exhausting. Your ability to advocate for your child depends on maintaining your own physical and emotional health. Seek support from other parents, counselors, or Lyme disease support organizations.
Children cannot always tell us what is wrong. But they show us — through their behavior, their energy, their mood, their willingness to play. When a child changes, it is worth investigating why. Trust your instincts as a parent.

What to do if you suspect Lyme in your child

  1. Document the changes you have observed. Write down behavioral, cognitive, physical, and emotional changes with dates and context.
  2. Review exposure history. Consider tick exposure from outdoor activities, travel, camping, or simply playing in yards and parks in endemic areas.
  3. Seek medical evaluation. Start with your pediatrician but be prepared to seek specialized Lyme evaluation if standard assessment does not provide answers.
  4. Consider comprehensive testing. If standard tests are negative but clinical suspicion remains, specialized testing may be warranted.
  5. Reach out to us. If you would like a physician at Klinik St. Georg to review your child's case, you can share their story here. We understand the urgency parents feel, and we take every pediatric inquiry seriously.
