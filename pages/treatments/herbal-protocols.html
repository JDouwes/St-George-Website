Herbal protocols for Lyme disease

Reviewed by Julian Douwes M.D., Chief Medical Officer, Klinik St. Georg

Many Lyme disease patients use herbal antimicrobial protocols alongside or instead of pharmaceutical treatment. The Buhner, Cowden, and Zhang protocols are the most widely discussed. Here is a fair assessment of what we know, what remains unproven, and how herbals fit within a clinical treatment plan.

At a glance

Evidence level: Preliminary laboratory studies suggest certain herbal compounds have activity against Borrelia burgdorferi. However, large-scale clinical trials are lacking, and most evidence comes from in vitro research, case reports, and patient-reported outcomes.

Clinical role: Herbal protocols may offer supportive benefit alongside medical treatment. They are not recommended as standalone therapy for disseminated or late-stage Lyme disease.

Our position: We respect that many patients derive benefit from herbal protocols. We also believe that complex, chronic Lyme disease requires medical-grade intervention. Herbals can complement that, but should not replace it.

The major herbal protocols

The Buhner protocol

Developed by the late herbalist Stephen Harrod Buhner, this protocol is the most widely used herbal approach to Lyme disease. Buhner's framework targets not just Borrelia directly, but the immune dysregulation, inflammatory cascades, and collagen damage that chronic infection causes.

Core herbs in the Buhner protocol:

  • Japanese knotweed (Polygonum cuspidatum) contains resveratrol and emodin. Buhner considered it central to his protocol for its ability to cross the blood-brain barrier, its anti-inflammatory properties, and preliminary evidence of activity against Borrelia spirochetes and round body forms.
  • Cat's claw (Uncaria tomentosa) is used for its immunomodulatory effects. It may enhance immune function while simultaneously reducing inflammation, a balance that is important in chronic Lyme where the immune system is often both overactive and ineffective.
  • Andrographis has demonstrated broad-spectrum antimicrobial activity in laboratory settings and is used for its anti-spirochetal and immune-stimulating properties.

Buhner also recommended additional herbs targeting specific symptoms and co-infections, making his protocol customizable based on individual presentation. His books remain the primary reference for practitioners using this approach.

The Cowden protocol

Developed by Dr. William Lee Cowden, this protocol uses a proprietary combination of herbal extracts, primarily from South American plants, in a rotating schedule designed to prevent microbial adaptation.

The protocol typically includes samento (a form of cat's claw), banderol, cumanda, and several supporting herbs, administered in a specific rotation over several months. Proponents suggest the rotating approach addresses the tendency of Borrelia to develop tolerance to single antimicrobial agents.

A small pilot study published in 2007 reported symptom improvement in chronic Lyme patients using the Cowden protocol, but the study was limited in size and lacked a control group. No large-scale randomized trials have been conducted.

The Zhang protocol

Dr. Qingcai Zhang developed a protocol drawing on traditional Chinese medicine principles, using a combination of Chinese herbal formulas designed to address what he describes as the multiple forms and life stages of Borrelia. His protocol includes AI#3 (Artemisia annua-based), HH (Houttuynia-based), and several other proprietary formulations.

The Zhang protocol is less widely used than the Buhner or Cowden protocols and has even less published clinical evidence. However, some Lyme-literate practitioners incorporate Chinese herbal medicine principles into their treatment approach.

Common herbs and their evidence

Beyond the structured protocols, several individual herbs have been studied for activity against Borrelia:

Cryptolepis sanguinolenta showed the most potent activity against Borrelia burgdorferi in a 2020 Johns Hopkins study (Feng et al.), outperforming several pharmaceutical antibiotics in vitro against stationary-phase Borrelia. This is one of the most promising findings in herbal Lyme research to date.

Japanese knotweed (Polygonum cuspidatum) also performed well in the same study, showing activity against both growing and stationary-phase Borrelia cultures.

Ghanaian quinine (Cryptolepis) and black walnut (Juglans nigra) have shown preliminary activity in laboratory settings, though clinical data remains limited.

Artemisia annua (sweet wormwood) is primarily used for its activity against Babesia, a common Lyme co-infection. It is the source of artemisinin, which is also used pharmaceutically for malaria.

It is important to distinguish between in vitro activity (what happens in a laboratory dish) and clinical efficacy (what happens in a patient). Many compounds that kill bacteria in the lab do not achieve adequate concentrations in human tissues, or have unacceptable side effects at effective doses. The gap between laboratory findings and clinical proof remains significant for herbal Lyme treatments.

How herbals can complement medical treatment

For patients undergoing medical treatment for Lyme disease, herbal protocols may offer several supportive benefits:

  • Immune modulation: Herbs such as cat's claw and astragalus may help regulate immune function, which is often dysregulated in chronic Lyme.
  • Anti-inflammatory support: Japanese knotweed, turmeric (curcumin), and boswellia offer anti-inflammatory activity that may reduce symptom burden.
  • Antimicrobial breadth: Some herbs may have activity against Borrelia forms or co-infections that pharmaceutical antibiotics target less effectively.
  • Gut support: Certain herbs support GI health and may partially offset the microbiome disruption caused by antibiotic therapy.
  • Continuation of care: Herbal protocols can be maintained over longer periods than pharmaceutical antibiotics, potentially offering ongoing low-level antimicrobial and immune support after intensive medical treatment.

When herbal protocols are not enough

We respect the role of herbal medicine and the experience of patients who have benefited from these protocols. However, we also have a responsibility to be honest about their limitations.

Herbal protocols are unlikely to be sufficient as standalone therapy for:

  • Neuroborreliosis with active central nervous system involvement
  • Lyme carditis with conduction abnormalities
  • Severe, multi-system chronic Lyme disease with significant functional impairment
  • Cases involving multiple tick-borne co-infections requiring targeted pharmaceutical treatment
  • Patients who have used herbal protocols for extended periods without meaningful improvement

If you have been using herbal protocols for months without significant improvement, this does not mean the herbs failed. It may mean that the complexity of your illness has exceeded what herbal antimicrobials alone can address. A medical evaluation can help determine what additional interventions might be needed.

Our clinical perspective

At Klinik St. Georg, we neither dismiss herbal protocols nor recommend them as primary treatment for complex Lyme disease.

Patients who arrive with established herbal protocols are encouraged to discuss them with their medical team. In many cases, herbal support can continue alongside medical treatment. In some cases, specific herbs may need to be paused due to potential interactions with pharmaceutical agents or hyperthermia protocols.

Our focus remains on interventions with the strongest evidence base for addressing the core infection and the systemic dysfunction it causes: whole-body hyperthermia, targeted antimicrobials, immune modulation, and detoxification support. Herbal protocols are welcome as a complement to this approach, not a substitute for it.
