Questions to ask your Lyme doctor

20 essential questions to ask any Lyme disease practitioner before committing to treatment. Organised by category, with guidance on what good answers look like and what should concern you.

Why these questions matter

Choosing a Lyme disease doctor is one of the most consequential decisions you will make in your treatment journey. The right questions help you evaluate competence, transparency, and clinical approach—whether you are considering a local physician, an LLMD, or treatment abroad. These questions apply to any practitioner, including our own team at Klinik St. Georg.

About the doctor's experience

Question 1
How many Lyme disease patients have you treated?
Why it matters: Lyme disease is complex. A physician who has treated hundreds or thousands of Lyme patients will have seen the full spectrum of presentations and complications. Volume alone does not guarantee quality, but clinical experience with Lyme specifically is essential.
Question 2
What is your specific training or background in Lyme disease?
Why it matters: Lyme disease is not a standard topic in most medical training programmes. Look for physicians with additional training, conference participation, research involvement, or long-standing clinical focus on tick-borne diseases.
Question 3
How long have you been treating Lyme disease specifically?
Why it matters: Chronic Lyme treatment evolves. A physician with years or decades of experience will have refined their approach based on clinical outcomes, not just textbook theory.
Question 4
Do you treat co-infections, or do you focus only on Borrelia?
Why it matters: Many chronic Lyme patients also carry co-infections such as Babesia, Bartonella, Ehrlichia, or Anaplasma. A physician who only treats Borrelia may miss a significant piece of the clinical picture.
Question 5
Are you board-certified? In what specialty?
Why it matters: Board certification confirms a baseline of medical training and accountability. While Lyme-specific board certification does not exist, infectious disease, internal medicine, or integrative medicine certifications provide relevant foundations.

About the treatment approach

Question 6
What does your treatment protocol look like for a patient with my presentation?
Why it matters: A credible physician should be able to outline a general approach tailored to your situation. Vague answers or one-size-fits-all protocols should concern you.
Question 7
How long is the typical treatment course?
Why it matters: Treatment duration should be defined with milestones and endpoints, not open-ended. Ask what criteria are used to determine when treatment is complete or when the approach needs to change.
Question 8
What modalities do you use beyond antibiotics?
Why it matters: Antibiotics alone may not be sufficient for chronic Lyme. A multi-modal approach—combining antimicrobials with therapies that address inflammation, immune function, and detoxification—reflects a more comprehensive treatment philosophy.
Question 9
What does success look like, and how do you measure it?
Why it matters: Honest practitioners define success realistically. Look for answers that reference symptom improvement, functional recovery, and quality of life—not guaranteed cures.
Question 10
What happens if the initial treatment does not produce the expected improvement?
Why it matters: Treatment does not always work as planned. A good physician has a clear process for reassessing, adjusting protocols, and exploring alternative approaches when initial results are not satisfactory.

About diagnostic testing

Question 11
What tests do you run before starting treatment?
Why it matters: Treatment should be guided by thorough diagnostics. A baseline assessment should include Borrelia-specific testing, co-infection panels, inflammatory markers, and organ function tests.
Question 12
Which laboratory do you use, and why?
Why it matters: Standard commercial labs may miss chronic or late-stage infections. Specialty laboratories (such as ArminLabs, IGeneX, or in-house hospital panels) offer more sensitive testing for persistent tick-borne infections.
Question 13
Do you screen for co-infections, and which ones?
Why it matters: Co-infections can significantly complicate treatment and slow recovery. Comprehensive screening should cover Babesia, Bartonella, Ehrlichia, Anaplasma, Mycoplasma, and Chlamydia pneumoniae at minimum.
Question 14
How do you interpret a negative test result in a patient with classic Lyme symptoms?
Why it matters: Seronegative Lyme disease is well documented. A physician who dismisses persistent Lyme symptoms solely based on a negative standard test may not be the right fit for complex cases.
Question 15
How do you monitor treatment response during the programme?
Why it matters: Ongoing monitoring—including repeat blood work, symptom tracking, and clinical assessments—ensures that treatment is working and allows for timely adjustments.

About follow-up care

Question 16
What does your follow-up plan look like after treatment ends?
Why it matters: Treatment does not end on discharge day. A structured follow-up plan with scheduled check-ins, recommended testing intervals, and clear criteria for when to seek additional care is essential.
Question 17
Will you coordinate with my physician at home?
Why it matters: Especially important for patients travelling for treatment. Your treating physician should provide detailed documentation and be willing to communicate directly with your local doctor.
Question 18
How do I reach you if I have concerns after treatment?
Why it matters: Post-treatment concerns are normal. You should have a clear pathway to reach your medical team, whether by phone, email, or video consultation.
Question 19
Under what circumstances would you recommend a second round of treatment?
Why it matters: Some patients require additional treatment. A good physician can articulate the criteria that would warrant retreatment versus continued monitoring.
Question 20
What lifestyle changes or ongoing support do you recommend after treatment?
Why it matters: Recovery from chronic Lyme involves more than medical treatment. Nutrition, sleep, stress management, and immune support all play a role. A physician who addresses these factors takes a whole-patient approach.

Red flags in answers

When you ask these questions, pay attention not just to what is said but to how it is said. Here are answers that should concern you.

Answers that should raise concerns

  • "We guarantee a cure" — no responsible physician guarantees outcomes for chronic Lyme disease
  • "We do not need to test before starting" — treatment without diagnostics is guesswork
  • "Everyone gets the same protocol" — Lyme disease is too variable for one-size-fits-all treatment
  • Reluctance to discuss costs upfront — financial transparency is a sign of professional integrity
  • Pressure to commit before you are ready — no ethical clinic pressures patients into immediate decisions
  • Dismissal of your previous treatment — a good physician respects what has been tried and builds on it
  • No mention of co-infections — overlooking co-infections suggests a superficial approach
  • No follow-up plan — treatment without follow-up is incomplete treatment
