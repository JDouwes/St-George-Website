Red flags in Lyme treatment

How to identify warning signs when evaluating Lyme disease practitioners and clinics. What should concern you, what responsible treatment looks like, and how to protect yourself as a patient.

Why this matters

Chronic Lyme patients are vulnerable. Many have been dismissed by conventional medicine and are desperate for help. This desperation can make patients susceptible to practitioners who promise more than they can deliver. We publish this page because we believe informed patients make better decisions—even if that means scrutinising our own clinic with the same critical eye.

The seven warning signs

1. "Guaranteed cure" claims

No responsible physician guarantees a cure for chronic Lyme disease. The biology is too complex, individual responses too variable, and the honest medical answer is that outcomes depend on many factors including disease duration, co-infections, immune status, and prior treatment. Any practitioner who guarantees a cure is either misinformed or being dishonest.

What responsible practice looks like

A credible physician will discuss realistic expectations, define what improvement looks like for your specific case, and be honest about the range of possible outcomes. They will use language like "significant symptom improvement" or "functional recovery" rather than "guaranteed cure."

2. No diagnostic testing before treatment

Starting treatment without comprehensive diagnostics is guesswork. A proper evaluation should include Borrelia-specific testing, co-infection screening, inflammatory markers, organ function tests, and immune status assessment. If a practitioner wants to treat you without testing first, they cannot know what they are treating.

What responsible practice looks like

Thorough diagnostics before treatment begins. A clear explanation of what is being tested and why. Results shared with you openly, including unexpected or ambiguous findings.

3. Extremely long antibiotic-only protocols without monitoring

Extended antibiotic therapy without regular monitoring of treatment response, organ function, and potential side effects is dangerous. Months or years of antibiotics without periodic reassessment, blood work, and protocol adjustments suggest a passive approach that may cause harm.

What responsible practice looks like

Regular monitoring during antibiotic therapy. Periodic blood work to check liver and kidney function. Clear criteria for when to adjust, pause, or stop antibiotic therapy. Consideration of multi-modal approaches rather than relying on antibiotics alone.

4. No medical supervision during treatment

Any treatment that involves powerful medical interventions—whether IV antibiotics, hyperthermia, apheresis, or other therapies—requires qualified medical supervision. Unsupervised or minimally supervised treatment environments put patients at risk.

What responsible practice looks like

Treatment delivered in a licensed medical facility. Daily medical oversight by a qualified physician. Nursing staff present during procedures. Clear emergency protocols in place.

5. Pressure to commit to expensive packages upfront

High-pressure sales tactics have no place in medicine. If a clinic pushes you to commit immediately, offers "limited-time pricing," or requires full payment before you have received a personalised treatment plan and cost estimate, proceed with extreme caution.

What responsible practice looks like

A consultation before any financial commitment. A written, itemised cost estimate for your specific treatment plan. Time to consider and ask questions. No pressure to decide immediately.

6. No mention of co-infections or multi-system approach

Chronic Lyme disease rarely exists in isolation. Tick bites frequently transmit multiple pathogens simultaneously, and many chronic Lyme patients carry co-infections that require separate treatment strategies. A practitioner who focuses only on Borrelia without screening for or addressing co-infections may miss critical components of your illness.

What responsible practice looks like

Comprehensive co-infection screening as part of the initial diagnostic workup. A treatment plan that addresses the full clinical picture, not just one pathogen. Recognition that symptom persistence may indicate untreated co-infections.

7. No follow-up plan or post-treatment support

Treatment does not end on discharge day. A practitioner who provides treatment but no follow-up plan, no monitoring protocol, and no pathway for ongoing support is providing incomplete care.

What responsible practice looks like

A written follow-up plan with recommended testing intervals. Remote consultation availability. Coordination with your local physician. Clear guidance on when to seek additional care.

How to evaluate any clinic objectively

Checklist for evaluating a Lyme treatment provider

  • Is the facility a licensed medical institution, or a private practice?
  • Are the treating physicians board-certified in a relevant specialty?
  • How many years has the clinic been treating Lyme disease specifically?
  • How many Lyme patients has the clinic treated?
  • Does the clinic publish its treatment approach transparently?
  • Are costs discussed openly before any commitment?
  • Can you speak with former patients or read verified testimonials?
  • Does the clinic acknowledge limitations and realistic outcomes?
  • Is the clinic willing to coordinate with your physician at home?
  • Does the clinic welcome your questions, including difficult ones?

Apply these criteria to every clinic you consider, including ours. A clinic that resists scrutiny is not one you should trust with your health.
