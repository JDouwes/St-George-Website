Lyme treatment in Germany vs. the United Kingdom

A fair comparison of what each country offers for Lyme disease patients. NHS limitations, NICE guidelines, treatment availability in Germany, cost differences, and what the practical journey looks like for UK patients.

Disclosure: This article is published by Lyme Treatment Germany, an educational platform operated in partnership with Klinik St. Georg in Bad Aibling, Germany. We present both countries' approaches as fairly as we can and label editorial assessments clearly. Learn about our editorial approach.
Quick answer

The NHS provides effective acute Lyme treatment but offers limited options for patients with persistent symptoms. NICE guidelines restrict treatment duration and do not recognise chronic Lyme disease as a diagnosis. Germany offers additional modalities—including whole-body hyperthermia, therapeutic apheresis, and hospital-based integrative protocols—that are not available through the NHS. The short flight from the UK (2 hours from London to Munich) makes Germany the most accessible international option for British patients seeking expanded treatment.

What the NHS offers for Lyme disease

The NHS provides free-at-point-of-care treatment for Lyme disease, and for early-stage cases, this care is effective. However, the system has well-documented limitations for patients whose symptoms persist.

NICE guidelines

The National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) published its Lyme disease guideline (NG95) in 2018. Key provisions include a standard 21-day course of doxycycline for most presentations, IV ceftriaxone for neurological Lyme, and no recommendation for retreatment if symptoms persist after the initial course.

NICE does not recognise "chronic Lyme disease" as a diagnosis. Patients with persistent symptoms after standard treatment are classified as having "post-infectious Lyme disease" or are referred for symptom management rather than further antimicrobial therapy.

Where UK patients get stuck

Common frustrations reported by UK patients

  • Limited treatment duration — the NHS typically offers one course of antibiotics; extended or repeated courses are difficult to obtain
  • Diagnostic challenges — the two-tier testing system (ELISA followed by immunoblot) can miss late-stage or chronic infections
  • Dismissal of persistent symptoms — patients report being told their symptoms are psychological or unrelated to Lyme after standard treatment
  • No access to advanced modalities — hyperthermia, apheresis, and integrative Lyme protocols are not available through the NHS
  • Long referral wait times — specialist infectious disease appointments can take months
We acknowledge that the NHS provides excellent acute Lyme care and is free at the point of use. These limitations apply specifically to patients with chronic or treatment-resistant presentations.

Treatment availability comparison

United Kingdom (NHS)

  • Oral antibiotics — 21-day doxycycline; free on the NHS
  • IV antibiotics — available for neuroborreliosis; hospital referral required
  • Retreatment — not routinely offered after initial course
  • Hyperthermia — not available
  • Apheresis — not used for Lyme disease
  • Private Lyme clinics — exist but are unregulated and vary widely in quality

Germany (Klinik St. Georg)

  • Oral and IV antibiotics — tailored to infection profile and co-infections
  • Whole-body hyperthermia — certified medical procedure with 30+ years of clinical experience
  • H.E.L.P. apheresis — removes inflammatory mediators and immune complexes
  • Advanced diagnostics — comprehensive panels including co-infection screening
  • Integrative protocols — IV therapies, photodynamic therapy, immune modulation
  • Hospital-based care — 2–3 week inpatient program with daily medical oversight

Cost comparison

Getting from the UK to Germany

Germany is the most convenient international treatment destination for UK patients. The journey is shorter than many domestic UK trips.

Flight options

  • London (Heathrow/Gatwick/Stansted) — 1 hour 50 minutes to Munich. British Airways, Lufthansa, easyJet, Ryanair.
  • Manchester — 2 hours direct to Munich (Lufthansa, seasonal services) or 3–4 hours via connection
  • Edinburgh — 2.5 hours direct (seasonal) or 3–4 hours via London or Frankfurt
  • Birmingham — 2 hours direct to Munich (Lufthansa) or connection via Frankfurt

Typical return fare: £80–£400 depending on airline and booking lead time. Budget carriers frequently offer fares under £150 return.

Post-Brexit travel requirements

UK citizens can enter Germany and the Schengen Area for up to 90 days within any 180-day period without a visa. You need a valid UK passport (valid for at least three months beyond your planned stay). No medical visa is required. Travel insurance is recommended but not mandatory for entry.

Time zone

Germany is 1 hour ahead of the UK (GMT+1 / CET). There is virtually no jet lag, and you can travel on the morning of your appointment day if needed.

Post-treatment follow-up with your UK GP

Coordinating care between Germany and the UK is straightforward because of the proximity, shared medical standards, and English-language documentation.

How follow-up works

  • You receive a comprehensive discharge summary in English detailing your diagnosis, treatment, and follow-up plan
  • Your GP can order recommended blood tests and monitoring through the NHS
  • Remote video consultations with your German medical team are available for ongoing questions
  • If your GP is unfamiliar with post-hyperthermia protocols, the German team can provide a clinician-to-clinician briefing
  • Some patients use a UK private GP for follow-up if their NHS GP is not supportive

Why UK patients seek treatment in Germany

Editorial assessment

Based on our clinical experience with British patients, the most common reasons include:

  • Exhausted NHS options — completed standard treatment but symptoms persist, with no further NHS pathway available
  • Seeking advanced modalities — whole-body hyperthermia and apheresis are not available anywhere in the UK
  • Diagnostic depth — comprehensive co-infection screening and advanced testing panels not routinely available on the NHS
  • Defined treatment timeline — a 2–3 week intensive programme rather than months of fragmented outpatient care
  • Proximity and ease of travel — a 2-hour flight, no visa required, minimal jet lag, and English-language care
This assessment reflects our editorial perspective informed by our partnership with Klinik St. Georg. If you are in the early stages of Lyme disease, NHS treatment may be entirely sufficient. Germany is most relevant for patients with chronic or treatment-resistant presentations.
