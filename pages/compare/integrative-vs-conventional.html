Integrative vs. conventional Lyme treatment

What these terms actually mean, how the approaches differ, when conventional treatment is sufficient, and when a multi-modal integrative approach may offer more for chronic and treatment-resistant Lyme disease.

Disclosure: Klinik St. Georg practices evidence-based integrative medicine. This comparison is written from that perspective. We have attempted to represent the conventional approach fairly and acknowledge its strengths. Learn about our editorial approach.
Quick answer

Conventional treatment follows IDSA guidelines: short-course antibiotics, acknowledgement of acute Lyme, scepticism toward chronic Lyme as a persistent infection. Integrative treatment, as practised at Klinik St. Georg, acknowledges persistent infection, uses antibiotics as a foundation, and adds modalities like whole-body hyperthermia, apheresis, and immune support. It is not "alternative" medicine—it is evidence-based medicine that uses a broader toolkit.

The conventional approach

Conventional Lyme treatment is based primarily on guidelines from the Infectious Diseases Society of America (IDSA) and, in the UK, the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE).

Core principles

  • Standard antibiotic courses — 14–28 days of doxycycline, amoxicillin, or ceftriaxone depending on disease stage and presentation
  • Acute focus — treatment is most effective when initiated early after infection
  • "Post-treatment Lyme disease syndrome" — persistent symptoms after standard treatment are attributed to residual inflammation or immune dysregulation, not ongoing active infection
  • Against retreatment — IDSA guidelines generally do not recommend additional antibiotic courses after standard treatment has been completed
  • Evidence standard — treatment recommendations are based on randomised controlled trials and systematic reviews

When conventional treatment works well

For early-stage Lyme disease—diagnosed promptly after a tick bite, ideally with an EM rash—conventional antibiotic treatment is highly effective. The vast majority of patients treated early recover fully. Conventional medicine deserves credit for this. If you are in the early stages of Lyme disease, standard antibiotic therapy should be your first course of action.

The integrative approach

Integrative Lyme treatment builds on conventional antimicrobial therapy and adds additional modalities that address the disease from multiple angles simultaneously.

What "integrative" actually means

The term "integrative" is sometimes confused with "alternative." They are not the same.

Alternative medicine

  • Replaces conventional treatment entirely
  • May reject evidence-based medicine
  • Often lacks clinical evidence
  • No medical supervision required

Integrative medicine

  • Builds on conventional treatment as a foundation
  • Adds clinically supported modalities
  • Uses evidence to guide selection of therapies
  • Delivered under medical supervision in clinical settings

What integrative Lyme treatment includes

Multi-modal treatment components

  • Antimicrobial therapy — targeted antibiotics remain a core component, tailored to infection profile and co-infections
  • Whole-body hyperthermia — raising core body temperature to exploit the heat sensitivity of Borrelia bacteria, performed with certified medical equipment
  • Therapeutic apheresis — H.E.L.P. apheresis to remove inflammatory mediators, immune complexes, and toxins from the blood
  • IV therapies — intravenous vitamin C, glutathione, and other agents to support immune function and detoxification
  • Photodynamic therapy — IV laser therapy to enhance immune response and antimicrobial activity
  • Immune modulation — therapies aimed at rebalancing immune function disrupted by chronic infection

When each approach is appropriate

Editorial assessment

Conventional treatment is likely sufficient when:

  • You have been recently diagnosed with early-stage Lyme disease
  • You have a confirmed EM rash and prompt diagnosis
  • You have not yet tried standard antibiotic therapy
  • Your symptoms are localised and have not become systemic

Integrative treatment may be appropriate when:

  • You have completed standard antibiotic treatment but symptoms persist
  • You have been living with chronic Lyme for months or years
  • You have multiple co-infections complicating your clinical picture
  • Antibiotic therapy alone has not produced adequate improvement
  • You have systemic symptoms affecting multiple organ systems
  • Your immune function appears compromised by chronic infection
This assessment reflects our clinical perspective. We encourage patients to seek multiple opinions and to start with conventional treatment if they have not yet tried it.

Where Klinik St. Georg fits

Editorial

Klinik St. Georg practices what we describe as evidence-based integrative medicine. This means we use antibiotics as part of our treatment foundation—we do not reject conventional medicine. But we also recognise that antibiotics alone are often insufficient for chronic Lyme disease, and we add modalities with clinical support: whole-body hyperthermia, therapeutic apheresis, IV therapies, and immune modulation.

We are not an "alternative" clinic. We are a licensed German hospital with board-certified physicians, certified medical equipment, and 30 years of clinical experience treating Lyme patients. Our approach is multi-modal because chronic Lyme disease is a multi-system illness that benefits from treatment addressing multiple mechanisms simultaneously.

We also believe in honest limitations. Integrative treatment is not a magic solution. Not every patient responds equally. We discuss realistic expectations before treatment, monitor progress throughout, and adjust our approach based on clinical response.
