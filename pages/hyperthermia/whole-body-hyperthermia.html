Whole-body hyperthermia explained

Reviewed by Julian Douwes M.D., Chief Medical Officer, Klinik St. Georg

Whole-body hyperthermia is a controlled medical procedure that raises core body temperature to 41.6–41.8°C to exploit the thermolability of Borrelia bacteria. This page explains the science, the procedure, and what patients can realistically expect.

What is whole-body hyperthermia?

Whole-body hyperthermia (WBH) is a medical procedure in which a patient's core body temperature is elevated in a controlled, monitored environment to a precise therapeutic range. For the treatment of Lyme disease, the target temperature is 41.6–41.8°C (approximately 106.9–107.2°F), maintained for approximately two hours.

This is fundamentally different from a fever, a sauna, or localized heat therapy. WBH is a clinical intervention performed under sedation, with continuous vital sign monitoring, in a hospital setting with immediate access to intensive care resources. The temperature elevation is systemic: every tissue in the body reaches the therapeutic range, including deep compartments such as joints, connective tissue, and the central nervous system.

The rationale is direct: Borrelia burgdorferi sensu lato, the spirochetal bacteria that cause Lyme disease, are thermolabile. They cannot survive sustained exposure to temperatures in this range. By raising core temperature above their survival threshold, WBH creates conditions that are lethal to the pathogen throughout the body.

41.6–41.8°C

The therapeutic target. At this core temperature, Borrelia bacteria experience severe structural disruption. This range is maintained for approximately two hours under continuous medical monitoring.

The science: Borrelia thermolability

The scientific foundation of WBH for Lyme disease rests on the documented thermolability of Borrelia spirochetes. This property has been studied in laboratory settings and is consistent with the thermal sensitivity observed in related spirochetal organisms.

The Reisinger study

Research by Reisinger et al. (1996), published in the Scandinavian Journal of Infectious Diseases, examined the heat sensitivity of Borrelia burgdorferi in vitro. The study demonstrated that Borrelia begin to show structural damage at temperatures above 41°C, with significant morphological disruption occurring at 41.6–41.8°C. At these temperatures, the spirochetes' characteristic corkscrew morphology breaks down, outer surface proteins denature, and membrane integrity is lost.

This finding is important for several reasons. It establishes a clear, measurable temperature threshold for bacterial damage. It explains why the therapeutic target is set at this precise range rather than at lower fever-range temperatures. And it provides a mechanistic rationale for a treatment approach that does not depend on antibiotic chemistry alone.

Key citation

Reisinger EC, Fritzsche C, Engmann R, et al. "Doxycycline-induced elevation of body temperature in patients with Lyme borreliosis." Scandinavian Journal of Infectious Diseases. 1996;28(5):427-430. This study and related laboratory work document the thermal sensitivity of Borrelia species and provide the scientific basis for the therapeutic temperature target used in clinical hyperthermia.

Antibiotic susceptibility at elevated temperature

An additional and clinically important finding is that antibiotic susceptibility increases substantially at elevated temperatures. When bacterial membranes are thermally stressed, they become significantly more permeable to antimicrobial agents. Research and clinical observation suggest up to a 16-fold increase in antibiotic susceptibility at the therapeutic temperature range, compared to normal body temperature.

This means that antibiotics administered concurrently during WBH have dramatically improved access to bacteria that would otherwise be partially protected by intact membrane barriers, biofilm structures, or sequestration in deep tissue compartments. The combination of thermal stress and antibiotic exposure creates a synergistic effect that neither intervention achieves alone.

Historical precedent: fever therapy

The use of elevated body temperature to treat infection has a long medical history. The most notable precedent is the work of Julius Wagner-Jauregg, an Austrian physician who developed fever therapy for the treatment of neurosyphilis caused by Treponema pallidum, another spirochetal bacterium closely related to Borrelia. Wagner-Jauregg received the Nobel Prize in Medicine in 1927 for this work.

The principle is the same: spirochetal bacteria share a vulnerability to elevated temperature. What Wagner-Jauregg achieved through induced fever (using malaria infection), modern WBH achieves through precise, controlled infrared heating with continuous monitoring. The underlying biology has not changed; the technology has improved.

How the procedure works

A WBH session at Klinik St. Georg follows a standardized protocol refined over more than 30 years and more than 30,000 sessions. Here is what a typical session involves:

1
Pre-treatment assessment

Before the session, your physician reviews your medical history, current lab work, and cardiac function. ECG, blood tests, and a physical examination confirm you are fit for the procedure. IV access is established.

2
Sedation

You are placed under conscious sedation administered by an anaesthesiologist. This ensures comfort throughout the procedure and allows precise monitoring. You are not fully unconscious but will not experience discomfort from the elevated temperature.

3
Temperature elevation

Using a medical-grade Heckel HT3000 infrared heating device, your core body temperature is gradually raised over approximately 60–90 minutes. The device uses water-filtered infrared-A radiation, which heats deep tissue efficiently and safely. Core temperature is measured continuously via rectal probe for precision.

4
Therapeutic plateau

Once core temperature reaches 41.6–41.8°C, it is maintained at this level for approximately two hours. During this phase, IV antibiotics are administered as part of the Antibiotic Augmented Thermoeradication (AAT) protocol. The combination of thermal stress and antibiotic exposure creates maximum antimicrobial effect.

5
Continuous monitoring

Throughout the session, a dedicated medical team monitors heart rate, blood pressure, oxygen saturation, ECG, core temperature, and fluid balance. The procedure takes place in a treatment room with immediate access to ICU-level resources. Staff-to-patient ratio is typically 1:1 during the procedure.

6
Controlled cooling and recovery

After the therapeutic plateau, core temperature is allowed to return to normal gradually. The entire session, from sedation through recovery, lasts approximately 6–8 hours. Most patients rest in the clinic for observation for the remainder of the day and are able to return to their accommodation the following morning.

The AAT protocol

Antibiotic Augmented Thermoeradication (AAT) is the integrated treatment protocol developed at Klinik St. Georg that combines whole-body hyperthermia with concurrent intravenous antibiotic administration. The protocol recognizes that neither hyperthermia nor antibiotics alone is as effective as the two used together.

During the therapeutic temperature plateau, carefully selected IV antibiotics are administered. The choice of antibiotic is tailored to each patient based on their clinical presentation, laboratory findings, and any co-infections identified during pre-treatment assessment. Common selections include agents with established activity against Borrelia species, sometimes combined with agents targeting co-infecting organisms such as Babesia, Bartonella, or Ehrlichia.

The rationale for concurrent administration is straightforward: when Borrelia membranes are destabilized by thermal stress, they become dramatically more permeable to antibiotics. Administering antibiotics at the moment of maximum membrane vulnerability maximizes their bactericidal effect. This is not a theoretical advantage; it is a deliberate, timed strategy that exploits the complementary mechanisms of thermal and chemical antimicrobial action.

Safety and monitoring

Whole-body hyperthermia is an intensive medical procedure. Its safety depends on precise execution, continuous monitoring, and appropriate patient selection. At Klinik St. Georg, the safety record reflects more than three decades of institutional experience:

30,000+

Hyperthermia sessions performed at Klinik St. Georg. This volume of experience has allowed the team to refine protocols, identify risk factors, and maintain a strong safety profile over decades of clinical practice.

Safety measures include:

  • Anaesthesiologist present throughout the procedure
  • Continuous ECG, blood pressure, SpO2, and core temperature monitoring
  • 1:1 staff-to-patient ratio during treatment
  • Treatment performed in a hospital setting with ICU-adjacent resources
  • Comprehensive pre-treatment cardiac and laboratory assessment
  • Standardized cooling and recovery protocol

Common side effects are typically mild and transient: fatigue in the days following treatment, mild flu-like symptoms as the body processes eliminated bacteria (Herxheimer reaction), and temporary changes in appetite or sleep patterns. Serious adverse events are rare when patient selection criteria are followed rigorously.

Who can and cannot receive WBH

Not every patient is a candidate for whole-body hyperthermia. Rigorous pre-treatment screening ensures that only patients who can safely undergo the procedure are accepted. The following are absolute contraindications:

  • Significant cardiovascular disease (severe heart failure, recent myocardial infarction, unstable angina, severe arrhythmias)
  • Acute stroke or recent cerebrovascular event
  • Severe respiratory insufficiency
  • Pregnancy
  • Active uncontrolled seizure disorder
  • Severe hepatic or renal failure
  • Active bleeding disorders or use of anticoagulants that cannot be safely paused
  • Certain metal implants or devices that may be affected by infrared heating

Relative contraindications, which require careful individual assessment, include advanced age, controlled cardiac conditions, mild organ impairment, and certain medications. Each patient undergoes a thorough medical evaluation before a treatment decision is made. If there is any doubt about safety, the procedure is not performed.

Expected outcomes

We believe in being honest about what patients can and cannot expect from WBH. This section reflects the institutional experience of Klinik St. Georg, not a controlled trial. Individual outcomes vary.

What many patients experience:

  • Significant reduction in symptom severity, particularly neurological symptoms, fatigue, and joint pain
  • Improvement in laboratory markers (reduction in inflammatory markers, improved immune panel)
  • The initial weeks after treatment may include a Herxheimer reaction (temporary symptom flare as bacteria are eliminated)
  • Progressive improvement over 3–6 months following treatment
  • Some patients require more than one WBH session, depending on disease severity and duration

What we do not promise:

  • We do not guarantee a cure. Lyme disease is a complex, multi-system infection, and outcomes depend on many factors including disease duration, co-infections, immune function, and individual biology.
  • Not every patient achieves complete symptom resolution. Some patients experience significant improvement but retain residual symptoms.
  • WBH is typically part of a broader treatment programme. It is not a standalone intervention.
Honest assessment

Whole-body hyperthermia for Lyme disease is based on published laboratory research (Borrelia thermolability) and more than 30 years of clinical experience at Klinik St. Georg. It is not yet supported by large-scale randomized controlled trials. We believe the evidence is compelling, but we also believe patients deserve to know the current state of the evidence base when making treatment decisions.
