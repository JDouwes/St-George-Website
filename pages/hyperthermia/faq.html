Hyperthermia FAQ

Reviewed by Julian Douwes M.D., Chief Medical Officer, Klinik St. Georg

Straightforward answers to the questions patients most frequently ask about whole-body hyperthermia treatment for Lyme disease. If your question is not answered here, please contact us directly.

Safety and comfort

Whole-body hyperthermia has been used in clinical medicine for decades, primarily in oncology. At Klinik St. Georg, the procedure is performed under continuous medical supervision with real-time monitoring of core temperature, heart rate, blood pressure, oxygen saturation, and ECG. Serious adverse events are rare when performed in an appropriately equipped clinical setting by experienced medical teams.

Whole-body hyperthermia is not painful. Patients typically describe the experience as intense warmth, similar to being in a very hot sauna. Some patients experience mild discomfort from the sustained heat, and mild sedation is used to help patients remain comfortable throughout the procedure.

Patients receive mild sedation to ensure comfort during the procedure, but whole-body hyperthermia does not require general anesthesia. Most patients are in a relaxed, semi-conscious state during treatment. An anesthesiologist is present throughout to monitor the patient and adjust sedation as needed.

During whole-body hyperthermia, core body temperature is raised to approximately 41.6 to 42 degrees Celsius (approximately 107 degrees Fahrenheit) and maintained at that level for a controlled duration. This temperature range is above what the body can generate through natural fever but is within the range that has been used safely in clinical medicine for decades.

Hyperthermia may not be appropriate for patients with severe cardiovascular disease, uncontrolled epilepsy, recent stroke, pregnancy, certain bleeding disorders, or other conditions that make elevated body temperature medically risky. All patients undergo a thorough pre-treatment medical assessment to determine eligibility.

Treatment details

A typical treatment program includes 2 to 3 whole-body hyperthermia sessions over a 2 to 3 week period. The number of sessions is determined by the medical team based on your individual case, disease severity, and response to treatment. Some patients may benefit from additional sessions in a subsequent treatment cycle.

The total procedure typically takes several hours, including the gradual heating phase, the target temperature maintenance phase, and the controlled cooling phase. The time at target temperature varies based on the treatment protocol. Patients should expect to spend a full day at the clinic on treatment days, including pre-treatment preparation and post-treatment monitoring.

Efficacy and results

Hyperthermia targets Borrelia through a mechanism fundamentally different from antibiotics: direct thermal damage. Published research (Reisinger et al., 1996) demonstrated that Borrelia becomes structurally unstable at temperatures above 41.6 degrees Celsius. Clinical experience at Klinik St. Georg shows that many patients experience meaningful symptom improvement, though individual results vary and we do not promise cure.

Some patients achieve substantial improvement after a single treatment cycle. Others, particularly those with very long-standing disease or multiple co-infections, may benefit from a second treatment cycle several months later. This is assessed during post-treatment follow-up consultations. The need for repeat treatment does not indicate failure; it reflects the complexity of chronic Lyme disease.

Side effects and recovery

Common side effects include fatigue for 1 to 3 days following treatment, mild dehydration (managed with IV fluids), temporary skin flushing, and Herxheimer-like reactions (temporary worsening of symptoms as bacteria are killed). These effects are expected, monitored, and managed by the medical team. Serious side effects are uncommon in an appropriately supervised clinical setting.

Herxheimer-like reactions, a temporary worsening of symptoms caused by the release of bacterial toxins during die-off, are common and expected during effective Lyme treatment. The medical team monitors for these reactions and provides supportive care including IV fluids, detoxification support, and symptom management. While uncomfortable, Herxheimer reactions are generally considered a sign that treatment is affecting the infection.

Most patients feel tired and may experience mild flu-like symptoms for 1 to 3 days after a hyperthermia session. This is a normal response. Rest days are built into the treatment schedule between sessions. IV fluids and micronutrient support are provided during the recovery period. Most patients feel well enough to resume normal activities within 2 to 3 days of each session.

Preparation and logistics

Before arriving, patients receive specific preparation guidelines from the medical team. General recommendations include maintaining good hydration in the days before treatment, following an anti-inflammatory diet, continuing any prescribed medications unless directed otherwise, and arriving well-rested. Detailed preparation instructions are provided after your case is accepted.

The typical treatment program spans 2 to 3 weeks on-site at Klinik St. Georg in Bad Aibling. International patients should plan for approximately 3 weeks total, including travel days and initial recovery before the return journey. Some patients choose to extend their stay to allow additional recovery time.

We recommend waiting at least 2 to 3 days after your final hyperthermia session before undertaking long-haul air travel. The medical team will advise you based on your individual recovery. Adequate hydration during travel is important, and we provide post-treatment travel guidance for all international patients.

Treatment costs vary based on the scope of your individualized treatment plan, including the number of hyperthermia sessions, diagnostic testing, and additional therapies. A detailed cost estimate is provided after the initial case review and before any commitment to treatment. Hyperthermia for Lyme disease is generally not covered by standard health insurance, though some private insurers may provide partial coverage.
