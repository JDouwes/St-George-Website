Bringing a companion

Having someone by your side during treatment makes a real difference. Here is what companions can expect and how we welcome them into the process.

We strongly encourage patients to bring a companion—a spouse, partner, parent, sibling, or close friend. In our experience, patients who have emotional support during treatment tend to recover more smoothly and manage the difficult days with greater resilience. Your companion is not just a visitor. They are part of your healing.

What companions can expect

Being a companion during a treatment stay is a unique experience. It is not a vacation, but it is also not as overwhelming as many companions fear before they arrive.

Visiting hours

The clinic maintains generous visiting hours. Companions are welcome throughout much of the day, and the staff understands that flexibility matters when your partner is going through intensive treatment. On treatment days, there will be periods when the patient needs rest and medical privacy, but you will have ample time together.

Involvement in care discussions

With the patient's consent, companions are welcome to sit in on physician consultations. Many patients find it helpful to have someone else listening, taking notes, and asking questions. Physicians are accustomed to including companions in discussions about treatment plans, progress, and what to expect next.

Daily rhythm

Your days will follow a natural rhythm: mornings at the clinic, time during treatments for yourself, afternoons and evenings with the patient. Some companions find this rhythm surprisingly restorative—a chance to step out of their own daily pressures and focus on something that matters deeply.

A companion's typical day

1

Morning

Breakfast at your accommodation, then walk or drive to the clinic. Spend time with the patient before their scheduled treatments begin. Some companions bring coffee and spend the first hour of the day together quietly.

2

Mid-morning to early afternoon

While the patient is in treatment sessions, you have free time. This is when companions typically explore Bad Aibling, visit the thermal baths, take walks along the Mangfall river, work remotely from a cafe, or simply rest. If you need to maintain work commitments, the town has Wi-Fi-friendly cafes and your accommodation will have reliable internet.

3

Afternoon

Return to the clinic to be with the patient during their recovery and rest period. This is often a good time for quiet conversation, reading together, or simply being present. On days when the patient feels well, you might take a short walk on the clinic grounds together.

4

Evening

Dinner together—either at the clinic or at a local restaurant on days when the patient feels up to it. Evenings tend to be quiet. Many companions describe developing a peaceful routine that they did not expect to find during a medical trip.

Where companions stay

Companions stay in separate accommodation near the clinic. Bad Aibling offers several comfortable options.

Accommodation options

  • Local hotels and guesthouses: €60–€120 per night, often with breakfast included
  • Furnished apartments and Airbnb: €50–€90 per night, with kitchen facilities for longer stays
  • Nearby towns (Kolbermoor, Rosenheim): additional options if Bad Aibling availability is limited

The clinic's international patient coordinator can recommend specific places that previous companions have found comfortable and convenient. Booking early is recommended, especially during peak travel seasons. For more details, see our accommodation page.

Activities in and around Bad Aibling

While your primary reason for being here is to support your partner, the region offers enough to keep your free time meaningful and restorative.

In Bad Aibling

  • Therme Bad Aibling: modern thermal baths with heated pools, steam rooms, and saunas—an ideal way to decompress
  • Kurpark walks: peaceful, landscaped paths through the historic spa gardens
  • Town center cafes and bakeries: try a traditional Bavarian breakfast or afternoon coffee and cake
  • Weekly farmers' market: fresh produce, local cheese, bread, and Bavarian specialties
  • Local churches and historic buildings: quiet, beautiful architecture worth a short walk

Day trips (30–90 minutes)

  • Chiemsee: Bavaria's largest lake, with boat trips to the islands and Herrenchiemsee Palace (King Ludwig II's Versailles)
  • Munich: world-class museums, the Englischer Garten, Marienplatz, dining, and shopping—about 50 minutes by train
  • Salzburg: Mozart's birthplace across the Austrian border, with the fortress, old town, and Sound of Music sites—about 1 hour by car
  • Alpine hiking: trails in the foothills near Bayrischzell and Wendelstein, accessible by car or regional train
  • Rosenheim: a charming larger town with a pedestrian center, shops, and restaurants—15 minutes by car or train

The companion's role in recovery

The value of a companion goes beyond practical help. Your emotional presence during treatment has a tangible impact on recovery.

What patients need most

Patients frequently tell us that the most important thing their companion did was simply be there—not trying to fix things, not being endlessly optimistic, but being present and steady through the difficult days. On days when Herxheimer reactions are intense or fatigue is overwhelming, having someone who understands and does not need an explanation is powerful.

Taking care of yourself

Supporting someone through intensive medical treatment is emotionally demanding. Companions sometimes neglect their own well-being in the focus on the patient. Give yourself permission to take breaks, explore the area, and rest. You cannot sustain support for two to three weeks if you are running on empty. The clinic staff understands this and will never make you feel that stepping away for an afternoon is anything less than healthy.

After you return home

The companion's role continues after discharge. Recovery takes weeks to months, and having someone who witnessed the treatment—who understands what was involved and why patience is needed—makes the home recovery phase significantly easier for the patient. You become an advocate and an ally who can explain the process to family and friends who were not there.

If you are the companion reading this: thank you. The decision to set aside weeks of your life to support someone through treatment is not small. Your presence matters more than you probably realize, and the team at Klinik St. Georg will make sure you feel welcome and supported too.
