What to expect during treatment

A day-by-day look at your treatment stay—so you know exactly what each phase involves and can focus on what matters most: healing.

Every patient arrives with a mix of hope and apprehension. That is completely normal. Your treatment program is carefully structured, but it is also flexible enough to respond to how your body reacts. You will not be alone at any point—the team is with you every step of the way.

Your treatment timeline: week by week

A typical treatment program at Klinik St. Georg lasts two to three weeks. The exact duration and schedule depend on your individual case, but here is what the general arc looks like.

1

Days 1–3: Arrival and comprehensive diagnostics

Your first days are focused on thorough diagnostic workup. This includes extensive blood panels, specialized Lyme and co-infection testing, imaging if indicated, and a full physical examination. Your physician will review all results with you and finalize your personalized treatment plan. These days are quieter—focused on understanding exactly what your body needs.

2

Days 4–7: Treatment begins

Active treatment starts with intravenous antibiotics, supportive infusions, and your first hyperthermia session. Your body begins responding. Some patients feel improvement early; others experience a temporary increase in symptoms as bacteria are disrupted. Both responses are expected and monitored closely.

3

Days 8–14: Core treatment phase

This is the most intensive period. You may have additional hyperthermia sessions, continued IV therapies, and integrative treatments such as ozone therapy, neural therapy, or detoxification support. Your physician adjusts the protocol based on your response, lab results, and how you feel day to day.

4

Days 15–21: Recovery and consolidation

The final phase focuses on recovery, stabilization, and preparation for going home. Treatments continue but may shift toward restoration—immune support, nutritional optimization, and building your post-treatment plan. Your physician will conduct a detailed discharge consultation covering next steps, follow-up schedule, and ongoing medications.

The hyperthermia session experience

Whole-body hyperthermia is the cornerstone of your treatment. Here is exactly what happens during a session, so there are no surprises.

Before the session

You will be prepared with intravenous access and monitoring equipment. Your physician will review your vital signs and confirm you are ready to proceed. You will receive sedation—most patients are in a light, comfortable sleep throughout the procedure. You will not experience pain.

During the session

Your core body temperature is gradually raised to approximately 41.6–42°C (107–108°F) using a specialized infrared system. The process takes several hours. Throughout, a dedicated medical team monitors your heart rate, blood pressure, oxygen levels, body temperature, and overall status continuously. IV antibiotics are administered at peak temperature, when bacteria are most vulnerable.

After the session

Your temperature is gradually brought back to normal. You will rest in a monitored recovery area as the sedation wears off. Most patients feel tired and a bit groggy for the remainder of that day and sometimes the following day. This is normal. Staff will check on you regularly, and fluids and nutrition are provided as you recover.

Patients frequently tell us that the anticipation of hyperthermia was far worse than the experience itself. Under sedation, you are not aware of the procedure. When you wake, it is already done.

Your daily routine

Treatment days follow a general rhythm, though your specific schedule will vary depending on which therapies are planned.

Typical daily schedule

  • Morning: vital signs, blood draws if scheduled, breakfast in your room or the dining area
  • Mid-morning: diagnostics appointments, lab reviews, physician consultations
  • Afternoon: treatment sessions—IV therapies, integrative treatments, or rest days
  • Late afternoon: free time for walking the grounds, reading, or resting
  • Evening: dinner, relaxation, and early rest

Your room

You will have a private room with an en-suite bathroom. Rooms are clean, comfortable, and quiet—designed for rest and recovery, not the sterile feeling of an acute hospital ward. You have access to Wi-Fi, and the clinic grounds offer paths for gentle walking when you feel up to it.

Meals

Nutritious meals are provided daily, prepared with attention to quality and dietary needs. If you have allergies, intolerances, or specific dietary requirements, the kitchen accommodates them. Many patients find that eating well during treatment contributes meaningfully to their energy and recovery.

Communication and support

Being in a German hospital as an international patient can feel unfamiliar. Here is how communication works.

English-speaking staff

The physicians treating you speak English fluently. Nursing staff and support personnel often speak English as well. For any situation where language is a barrier, the team will ensure clear communication—your understanding of your care is never left to chance.

Physician rounds

Julian Douwes M.D. and the clinical team conduct regular rounds. You will see your physician daily or near-daily during your stay. These are not brief check-ins—your physician will review your progress, discuss how you are feeling, explain any changes to your treatment plan, and answer your questions. If your companion is present, they are welcome to participate.

Reaching the team

You have a call button in your room at all times. Nursing staff are available around the clock. If you have concerns between physician visits—about a new symptom, medication side effects, or anything else—you can raise them immediately. There is no threshold of severity required to ask for help.

The emotional and physical landscape

Treatment is not a straight line from illness to wellness. Knowing what to expect emotionally and physically helps you navigate the difficult days without losing perspective.

Herxheimer reactions

When treatment effectively disrupts bacteria, your body processes the resulting toxins. This can temporarily worsen symptoms—increased fatigue, joint pain, headaches, brain fog, or flu-like feelings. This is called a Herxheimer reaction, and while uncomfortable, it is often a sign that the treatment is reaching the infection. Your physician will help you manage these reactions with supportive therapies.

Fatigue days

Some days, especially after hyperthermia sessions, you will feel deeply tired. This is expected. Rest is not a sign of failure—it is part of the healing process. The schedule accommodates recovery days, and no one will expect you to power through exhaustion.

Improvement days

Many patients experience moments of clarity, reduced pain, or improved energy during their stay. These may come in waves. Some improvement is noticeable during treatment; for many patients, the most significant gains come in the weeks and months after discharge. Your physician will help you set realistic expectations.

Patients often describe the treatment stay as one of the hardest and most hopeful experiences of their lives. The bad days are real. But so are the turning points. The team is there for both.
